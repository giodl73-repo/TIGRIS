# UPSTAGE — Concept

## Premise

You are in a scene. You did not choose to be in a scene. You were pushed into one.

UPSTAGE is a 4–8 player party game about stealing the spotlight. Each player holds
a hand of Role cards — Lead, Villain, Comic Relief, Ingénue, Chorus. On your turn you
perform a scene from your current role: a gesture, a line, a reaction, a pause. The
audience watches. If anyone holds an UPSTAGE reaction card whose trigger matches
something in your performance — a word type, a physical gesture, a direction faced —
they leap up, physically move to you, and take the scene. Your performance stops.
Theirs begins.

The player who upstaged you is now performing. And they can be upstaged too.

The scene ends when everyone who can upstage has upstaged, or when the scene naturally
closes (a timer, a held note, a deliberate bow). Whoever holds the stage at the close
scores. But the player who upstaged the most dramatically — judged by the room — scores
the Star token. The Star token is worth more than any scene.

UPSTAGE is competitive and social and physical and funny. It is not trying to be a
euro game. It is trying to be the game that happens after dinner when someone says
"should we play something?" and everyone says yes.

## Players and length

- **Players:** 4–8
- **Length:** 20–30 min
- **Weight:** 1.0

## Anchor mechanic

The UPSTAGE reaction. Your UPSTAGE card sits face-up on the table in front of you.
It shows a trigger — a physical cue from the performing player that gives you
permission to steal the scene. When you see it, you stand up, cross to the performer,
and take over. No voting. No asking. You just go. The trigger is specific enough to
be fair (not "anything they do") and broad enough to fire frequently (not "left hand
raised during the word 'fire'"). The social contract: once you upstage, you commit.
You cannot half-upstage. You cannot upstage and then sit down. You are in the scene
now. A26 fires because the implicit norm — commit fully or don't go — is unwritten
but absolute.

## Artifact-as-story

**The Role deck.** 50 cards across 5 role types (10 each: Lead, Villain, Comic Relief,
Ingénue, Chorus). Each card has a two-line scene description — not a script, a
direction. "You are receiving devastating news. You have been waiting for it." "You
have just realized you are the only one who finds this funny." Each is specific enough
to launch a performance and open enough to be stolen from. The deck is the game's
content engine: the variety of role combinations across 50 cards makes every scene
different.

Secondary artifact: **the UPSTAGE reaction cards** (20 cards, triggers for specific
performance cues). Distributed one face-up per player each round. Your trigger changes
each scene — you never know quite when you'll leap up.

## Inspiration / lineage

- **Vlaada Chvátil — Codenames (2015):** The word-association social contract — give a
  clue that your team understands without saying it plainly. UPSTAGE's performance
  triggers are the same: commit to something specific enough to be read, not so
  specific it can't be stolen.
- **Donald Vaccarino — Dominion (2008):** Reaction cards that fire involuntarily during
  others' turns. UPSTAGE takes the Moat mechanic and turns it into a physical act.
- **Charades (folk game):** The performance as communication. UPSTAGE borrows the
  physical body as medium but removes the guessing — you're not conveying information,
  you're conveying *presence*.
- **Theatresports / improv tradition:** The "yes, and" contract; the commitment to a
  scene once you enter it; the rule that you cannot deny what has been established.
  UPSTAGE's "you cannot half-upstage" is the improv "yes, and" made into a game rule.

Novelty claim: **No designed party game makes physical scene-stealing the primary
scoring mechanic.** Improv games exist but have no score. Charades has performance
but no interruption. UPSTAGE makes the interruption the game.

## Non-goals

- Not a euro game. No resource economy. No conversion chains. No end-game scoring
  calculation.
- Not a cooperative game. The Star token is contested; someone wins.
- Not a scripted improv game. The Role cards give direction, not dialogue. Players
  find their own words and gestures.
- Not a judged performance game (no American Idol panel). The Star token is judged
  by the room all at once — everyone simultaneously points at who they think was the
  star. Plurality wins.
- Not a long game. If UPSTAGE is taking more than 30 minutes something is wrong.

## Open questions (to resolve during DESIGN)

1. **Trigger design:** How specific is a trigger? Options: (a) "any physical gesture
   with the left hand," (b) "turns their back to the audience," (c) "says a word
   beginning with a specific letter." Test (b) first — physical, unambiguous, funny.

2. **Scene closing mechanic:** What ends a scene? Options: (a) 90-second timer,
   (b) the performer bows deliberately, (c) no more UPSTAGE cards fire in 10 seconds.
   Test (c) — most dramatic; scene ends when the room goes quiet.

3. **Star token judging:** Simultaneous point at who was the star? Or majority vote
   with raised hands? Simultaneous point is faster and more social.

4. **Scoring:** Scenes scored by who holds stage at close. Star token: 1 per game,
   awarded at end. Does the Star override scene scores or add to them? Start with:
   Star = 3 VP, scene close = 1 VP. Most VP wins.

5. **UPSTAGE card distribution:** One trigger card face-up per player per scene, or
   a hand of 2 (choose which to reveal)? Start with 1 face-up — simpler, faster.

6. **8-player calibration:** At 8p, scenes could get chaotic (multiple simultaneous
   upstages). Rule: only one upstage fires at a time — first physical arrival at the
   performer wins. Test this; it may need a 3-second "claim window."
