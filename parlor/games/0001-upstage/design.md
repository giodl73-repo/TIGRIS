# UPSTAGE — Rules

## 1. Overview & Object of the Game

Somebody is performing. You can take it from them.

UPSTAGE is a 4–8 player party game about stealing the scene. Each round, one player
holds the stage and performs a short dramatic moment drawn from a Role card. The other
players hold face-up UPSTAGE cards — each showing a specific physical trigger. When
the performer hits your trigger, you must stand up, cross to them, and take the scene.
No asking. No voting. You just go. The scene continues with the new performer.

The game runs in scenes. A scene ends when it naturally closes — no upstage fires for
5 seconds, or the performer takes a deliberate bow. Whoever holds the stage at close
scores. After all scenes are played, the room votes for the Star token: one player
whose single upstage moment was the most memorable.

The social contract of UPSTAGE: if your trigger fires, you commit. Half-upstaging —
standing up, walking halfway, then sitting back down — is the one thing the game
cannot survive. The room has to trust that when someone crosses the floor, they mean it.

Most VP at game end wins. The Star token is worth 3 VP — often more than any single
scene. The player who upstaged most boldly beats the player who scored most safely.

## 2. Components

**Role cards — 50 cards (10 per role type)**
Standard playing-card size. Each shows:
- Role name (top): LEAD / VILLAIN / COMIC RELIEF / INGÉNUE / CHORUS
- Scene direction (body, 2 lines): a specific dramatic situation, not a script

Sample Role card directions:

| Role | Example direction |
|---|---|
| LEAD | "You have just been told you are the last hope. You didn't ask for this." |
| LEAD | "You are saying goodbye for what might be the last time. You won't show it." |
| VILLAIN | "You are winning, and you want everyone in the room to know it." |
| VILLAIN | "You have just discovered something that proves you were right all along." |
| COMIC RELIEF | "You think this situation is completely fine. You are the only one." |
| COMIC RELIEF | "You've been trying to get someone's attention for a very long time." |
| INGÉNUE | "You just understood something obvious that everyone else figured out hours ago." |
| INGÉNUE | "You need to ask a question but you're not sure this is the right moment." |
| CHORUS | "Something important is happening behind you. You're the only one who sees it." |
| CHORUS | "You were supposed to have one line. You've decided that was a mistake." |

**UPSTAGE cards — 24 cards (triggers)**
Same size as Role cards. Each shows one physical trigger — a specific observable action
that the performer might do. Held face-up in front of each player at all times during
a scene. Triggers are physical and unambiguous:

| # | Trigger |
|---|---|
| 1 | Performer turns their back to the audience |
| 2 | Performer uses both hands simultaneously |
| 3 | Performer is silent for 3+ seconds |
| 4 | Performer points directly at someone |
| 5 | Performer kneels or lowers their level |
| 6 | Performer takes 3+ steps in one direction |
| 7 | Performer raises both arms above their head |
| 8 | Performer makes direct eye contact with you specifically |
| 9 | Performer laughs (in character or genuinely) |
| 10 | Performer names an object that isn't physically present |
| 11 | Performer addresses the audience directly |
| 12 | Performer whispers |
| 13 | Performer freezes in position |
| 14 | Performer sits down |
| 15 | Performer gestures toward a door or window |
| 16 | Performer repeats a word or phrase twice |
| 17 | Performer puts their hand over their heart |
| 18 | Performer shakes their head |
| 19 | Performer reaches out as if taking something |
| 20 | Performer smiles |
| 21 | Performer counts something (out loud or silently with fingers) |
| 22 | Performer turns to face a specific direction (not the audience) |
| 23 | Performer removes or adjusts an item of clothing |
| 24 | Performer bows or dips their head |

**Star token — 1**
A distinctive physical piece (larger than VP tokens; gold or bright color). Awarded
by room vote at game end. Worth 3 VP.

**VP tokens — supply**
Small discs or chips. 1 per scored scene. Each player collects their own.

**Direction cards — 12 (optional; see §9 Variants)**
Genre modifiers that change the scene's register. Shuffled and placed face-down.
Examples: "Make it a western." / "Everyone is underwater." / "In slow motion." /
"It's the happiest day of someone's life." / "Something is very wrong with the floor."

**Role reference cards — 5**
One per role type, showing 3 example energy notes for that role. Placed face-up in
the center for new players. Not required once the group knows the roles.

## 3. Setup

1. Shuffle the Role deck. Place face-down in the center — this is the **Stage deck**.
2. Shuffle the UPSTAGE deck. Place face-down separately.
3. Determine the first Stage Holder: whoever most recently performed in front of people
   (interview, karaoke, presentation, stage). Ties: player who laughs first at the
   question.
4. Place the Star token visibly to the side. It is not held by anyone yet.
5. Distribute VP tokens as a shared supply — no pre-dealing.
6. If using Direction cards (§9): shuffle and place face-down near the Stage deck.

---

## 4. Turn Structure

UPSTAGE plays in **scenes**, not turns. Each scene has four phases:

### Phase 1 — Deal
The Stage Holder draws 1 Role card from the top of the Stage deck and reads it
privately. They do not reveal the role type or the direction.

All other players each draw 1 UPSTAGE card and place it face-up in front of them,
visible to everyone at the table. Players may briefly read each other's triggers
aloud if desired ("mine is 'turns their back'") — this is allowed and often creates
anticipation.

### Phase 2 — The Scene
The Stage Holder stands up and performs. No time limit to begin — performers may
take a breath. The scene is live once the performer takes their first action.

The scene runs until it closes (see Phase 4). There is no time limit imposed during
the scene — the social pressure of holding the stage is the natural pacing mechanism.

### Phase 3 — The Upstage (may occur multiple times)
**Any player whose UPSTAGE trigger fires during the scene must immediately stand up,
cross to the Stage Holder's position, and take the scene.**

"Must" is the operative word. The trigger fires; you go. There is no decision.

Upstaging:
1. Stand up visibly — announce yourself by moving, not by speaking
2. Cross to the current Stage Holder's position (within arm's reach)
3. The original performer yields — they step aside or sit; their scene is over
4. The upstager is now the Stage Holder and continues the scene
5. Their Role card governs their performance — they may keep the same direction or
   play their own role's energy into the scene

**Multiple upstages:** If two players' triggers fire simultaneously, the first to
physically arrive at the Stage Holder takes the scene. The second player may sit or
attempt to upstage the new Stage Holder on their next trigger.

A player may only upstage once per scene (their UPSTAGE card is flipped face-down
after use). They draw a new card at the start of the next scene.

### Phase 4 — Scene Close
The scene ends when ONE of:
- **Natural close:** No upstage fires for 5 seconds AND the scene has run at least
  10 seconds total.
- **Bow:** The current Stage Holder performs a deliberate bow — head down, a clear
  voluntary close. Bowing ends the scene regardless of timing.
- **Hard cap:** The scene has run 90 seconds.

Whoever holds the stage at close scores 1 VP token from the supply.

### Reset
After scoring, all players discard used UPSTAGE cards (used cards go to a discard pile;
when the draw pile runs out, reshuffle). The Stage Holder for the NEXT scene is the
player to the LEFT of whoever started this scene as Stage Holder.

**Exception:** If a player upstaged and closed the scene as Stage Holder, THEY start
the next scene as Stage Holder (not the left rotation). Reward for successful upstaging.

## 5. Actions

UPSTAGE has three actions available to players. No action costs anything; the cards
are the resource.

### 1. Perform
*Available to: the current Stage Holder.*

Perform your Role card's direction in the space. No words are off-limits. No gestures
are off-limits. Props from the environment may be used. The performance is not timed
from the outside — the Stage Holder controls their own pace.

The only rule on Perform: honor your Role card. A Lead does not play for laughs; a
Villain does not play for sympathy. The role creates the scene's energy.

### 2. Upstage
*Available to: any player whose face-up UPSTAGE trigger fires.*

Stand up. Cross to the Stage Holder. Take the scene. This action requires no
announcement — the physical movement IS the announcement.

**The commitment rule:** Once you stand up, you must cross. Standing up and sitting
back down is a false upstage — a social contract violation the group should gently
enforce by name. "You stood up." The offending player must Cross.

### 3. Bow
*Available to: the current Stage Holder, at any time.*

A deliberate head-dip, a formal close gesture, a clear intentional ending. Bow ends
the scene immediately. Use this to close a scene that has found its peak moment — or
to escape a scene that has stalled.

## 6. Scoring / End Condition

### Scene VP
Each scene: 1 VP token to whoever holds the stage at close.

### Star Token
Awarded once at game end by room vote:
- All players simultaneously point at the player who had the single most memorable
  upstage moment of the entire game.
- Plurality wins. Player with the most points directed at them earns the Star token (3 VP).
- Ties: tied players split — the first tied player to claim the token gets it (reach,
  not rock-paper-scissors).
- A player who never upstaged cannot win the Star token even if voted for — the token
  requires at least one committed upstage in the game.

### End Trigger
When fewer Role cards remain in the Stage deck than the number of players, signal the
final scene: "One more." After that scene closes, game ends. Count VP. Vote for Star.

### Final Score
VP tokens + Star token (3 VP if won) = total. Most VP wins.

**Tiebreaker:** Most upstages performed across the game. Secondary: most scenes held
at close. Tertiary: Star token. If still tied: both players win — UPSTAGE is a party
game.

## 7. Edge Cases & Clarifications

**Q: Can you upstage someone who just upstaged you?**
Yes. There is no cooldown. If you yield the stage and then another player hits your
trigger from their new position — you may cross back. Scenes can chain.

**Q: What if the trigger is ambiguous — did they or didn't they point at someone?**
The player holding the trigger decides if their trigger fired. If they believe it did,
they go. If they chose not to go, they cannot retroactively claim the upstage after
the performer continues. The decision is immediate.

**Q: Can you upstage with your card face-down (already used)?**
No. Once your UPSTAGE card is face-down (used this scene), you cannot upstage again
until the next scene's deal.

**Q: What if nobody has a trigger card at all during a scene?**
Impossible in standard setup (each non-Stage-Holder player holds 1 trigger card per
scene). If a player somehow holds no card (e.g., they joined mid-game): they simply
watch this scene and draw for the next.

**Q: Does a Direction card change the Role card's direction?**
No — Direction cards add a layer on top of the existing Role card. The performer
plays their Role direction AND the scene's genre simultaneously.

**Q: Can two players start a scene as co-Stage-Holders?**
No. One Stage Holder per scene. The physical "who holds the stage" is always singular —
even if two performers are in the same space, whoever most recently upstaged holds it.

**Q: What if someone bows immediately (stalling)?**
A scene closed in under 10 seconds does not score. The hard floor of 10 seconds before
natural close or bow-score protects against deliberate stalling.

## 8. Special Subsystems

### The Star Token Vote

The Star token is the game's emotional climax. The vote happens after all scenes are
done, in the final 2 minutes of the game. Make it ceremonial:

1. All players recall the game's upstage moments (30 seconds of memory/discussion)
2. Countdown: "Three, two, one, VOTE" — all players point simultaneously
3. Tally by show of hands staying pointed
4. Award token

The discussion before the vote is encouraged and unmoderated. Players may campaign
for their own moment or another player's. This is not cheating — it's the game's
social layer.

### The False Upstage Penalty

The commitment rule (§5 Action 2) is enforced socially, not mechanically. The group
names false upstages when they see them. A suggested light penalty: a player who
false-upstages twice in one game loses 1 VP token (from their pile, returned to supply).

This rule is optional and should only be invoked if false upstages are genuinely
disrupting play. In most games the social pressure of "you stood up" is sufficient.

## 9. Variants

### Direction Cards (recommended for 2nd+ play)
At the start of each scene, flip the top Direction card face-up before the Stage
Holder draws their Role card. The Direction card governs the entire scene's genre or
modifier — all performers in this scene honor it. The Stage Holder draws their Role
card after seeing the Direction. Example scene: Direction "underwater" + Role card
"VILLAIN: you have just discovered something that proves you were right" = a villain
discovery scene performed as if submerged.

### Improv round (no Role cards)
Shuffle the Role deck away. Each scene is played without a direction — pure improv.
UPSTAGE cards still operate normally. Recommended for groups with improv experience.

### Audience mode (large groups 9-12)
Two players begin as Stage Holders (side by side). The rest are audience with UPSTAGE
cards. Either Stage Holder can be upstaged. The scene is scored based on who holds
the stage at close among ALL active performers.

### Scoring variant — Upstage bonus
Each successful upstage earns 0.5 VP (not just scene close). Rounds to nearest whole
at game end. This rewards committing to the upstage even if you don't close the scene.

## 10. Designer Notes

**Why "must" upstage?**
The commitment rule is the game's architecture. A trigger-based system where players
can choose whether to upstage degrades into waiting — everyone waits to see if the
performer is going to do something more interesting before spending their upstage.
Making the trigger mandatory converts a decision into a reaction. You're not choosing
to upstage; you're responding to the performer.

**Why no hidden triggers?**
Face-up UPSTAGE cards create anticipation in the audience — you know what could
trigger who, so the performer's choices carry social weight. A performer who turns
their back knows what they're risking. This converts UPSTAGE from a surprise mechanic
into a pressure mechanic: the audience is watching WITH information about what might
happen next.

**Why 50 Role cards and not 10?**
The directions on Role cards are the game's content engine. A small deck produces
repeated scenes quickly; a group playing multiple sessions needs variety. 50 cards
(10 per role) is approximately 5 full plays before repeat.

**Why the Star token over "most upstages wins"?**
Quantity-of-upstages scoring would optimize for tiny triggers that fire constantly and
scene-stealing for 3-second grabs. The Star token — voted by the room, worth 3 VP —
rewards the memorable moment, not the grinding accumulation. The player who crossed
the room in a way that made everyone gasp wins the Star. That player may have only
upstaged once.

## 12. Open Questions at DESIGN Stage

1. **Natural close timing:** 5-second silence before auto-close — is this too short
   (scenes close before they peak) or too long (scenes drag when stalled)? Test 5
   seconds vs. 3 seconds vs. player-count-matched (5 seconds at 4p, 3 seconds at 8p).

2. **UPSTAGE card count per player:** Current: 1 face-up card per non-Stage-Holder.
   Alternative: deal 2 cards per player and choose which to reveal. This adds a hand-
   management layer but raises weight slightly. Test 1 first.

3. **Star token timing:** Voted after all scenes, or voted after each scene for that
   scene's best moment? Per-scene voting adds ritual and keeps the Star alive throughout,
   but slows the game. Game-end vote is simpler. Test game-end.

4. **End trigger:** Role deck running low is the current trigger. Alternative: play
   exactly X scenes (player count × 3 scenes each feels about right for 25 minutes).
   Fixed scene count is more predictable. Test both.

5. **Role card secrecy:** Currently Stage Holder draws and keeps Role private. Would
   public Role cards (everyone can see the direction) create different dynamics?
   Probably less interesting — the audience's uncertainty about WHICH role the
   performer is playing is part of the reading. Keep private.

6. **Chorus role saturation:** At 8p, the Chorus role (background observer) may feel
   passive relative to the other 4 roles. Consider: Chorus performers get an extra
   UPSTAGE card (2 trigger cards) to compensate for the lower energy of their role.
   Test whether Chorus players feel underserved before implementing.

7. **8-player chaos management:** At 8p, 7 trigger cards are in play simultaneously.
   Multiple upstages firing at once could create a pile-on (4 players all crossing at
   once). Hard rule: only 2 upstages can chain in a single scene? Or first-arrival
   rule? First physical arrival is fastest and fairest — test this before imposing a
   chain limit.
