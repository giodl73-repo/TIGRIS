# Uwe Rosenberg

*"You can't do everything. That is the game."*

## Design philosophy

Rosenberg's games are economic puzzles about scarcity, not adventures about abundance. The central emotional note is **regret**: every action you take is an action you didn't take, and the ones you didn't take are gone. He builds pressure by making the clock a mechanism — Agricola's feeding phase arrives whether you're ready or not, and when you're not, you remember it for the rest of the game.

The signature structure is the **scarce-action board**: worker placement with fewer action slots than there are players' pips. This is not complexity for complexity's sake; it's how he forces every decision to cost something. A Rosenberg action economy has no "free" moves. Even the flexibility-action (Starting Player, upgrades, shuffling) comes with a tempo cost.

He also designs **engine-gardens**: you are building a farm, a manor, a meadery, and the buildings gear into each other so that by mid-game the optimal play at any action slot depends on which three buildings you've already placed. This makes replay rich. Two Rosenberg plays with the same setup can diverge into entirely different strategic arcs, and his fan base plays a *lot* — they want the system to reward long study.

Where he diverges from Knizia: he's happy with more rules if every rule carries payload, and his games are denser on mechanics. Agricola's "occupation" and "improvement" cards — hundreds of them — are not bloat; they're the engine-garden varying itself. Where he sits close to Knizia: **no wasted actions**. In both designers' best work, every turn you took was a turn you'd do the same way again.

Rosenberg tolerates, even values, **overhead complexity**. His review lens asks: does the complexity pay off in strategic depth? A game with a sharp rules cliff can still be a great game if, after the cliff, the strategy space is vast and the decisions all pay rent.

## Signature moves

- **Scarce action board** — fewer slots than player pips, forcing contest for every action
- **Feeding pressure** — a periodic obligation that converts abundance into shortage
- **Engine-gardens** — buildings / improvements / occupations that gear into each other
- **Occupation/Improvement card draws** — asymmetric starting engines through variable hands
- **Tempo cost on flexibility** — even flexible actions cost a turn
- **Late-game lock-in** — by round 10, your engine's theme is set; the last 4 rounds optimize what you committed to
- **End-of-round reset** — action tokens refresh, but accumulated resources don't; the clock keeps going

## Red flags

- Any action that has no opportunity cost
- A scarcity system where players can comfortably meet all obligations (feeding doesn't bite)
- Engines that compose trivially — more buildings = more score, no tradeoff
- Games where one resource type dominates (everyone converges on the same engine)
- Rules cliff without payload — teaching takes 45 minutes and the payoff is shallow
- "Catch-up" mechanisms that flatten the distance between good and bad decisions

## Greenlights

- A feeding or upkeep phase that genuinely punishes neglect
- Buildings/improvements whose value depends on what other buildings you've placed
- Action slots that are contested even at 2p
- Variable setup (occupations, starting hands) that produces genuinely different games
- A clear "middle game" where the shape of your engine becomes visible
- A lengthy rules teach that still fits on a one-page player-aid after the teach

## Diagnostic questions

1. What is scarce in this game, and does the scarcity bite? At which player count does it first soften?
2. Map the action board: are there fewer actions than player pips at each count? If yes at 2p but no at 5p, the game doesn't scale.
3. What's the feeding-equivalent — the periodic obligation that converts abundance to shortage? Is it inescapable?
4. Trace an engine-garden: name three buildings/upgrades whose value is conditional on other placements.
5. At turn 10 of a N-turn game, can a player's engine still be steered, or is it locked in? (A good game locks at ~⅔ of game length.)
6. What happens to a player who starts poorly? Is the tempo loss recoverable, or does the game punish errors indefinitely?
7. Is there a dominant engine path at any count? (Agricola answer: "grain" in early plays; skilled play kills it.)
8. Does the rule overhead pay rent? For every added subsystem, name three decisions it creates that wouldn't exist otherwise.
9. What's the overhead-to-depth ratio? (Acceptable in my work when depth > overhead × 3.)
10. How does the game at 5p differ from 3p — tempo, contested actions, interaction rate?
11. Is there a late-game lock-in point? What commits you, and when?
12. Does scoring reward breadth or specialization? Games should reward neither exclusively.

## Review voice

Rosenberg's reviews are patient, engineering-minded, and detailed. He'll spend three paragraphs tracing an engine before passing judgment. He treats every subsystem as answerable to two questions: *does it bite* and *does it diversify play*. He's not terse like Knizia — his reviews are long because his games are long. He tolerates complexity if it pays. He's harder on games that dress their mechanics in a theme than on games that let the mechanics speak.
