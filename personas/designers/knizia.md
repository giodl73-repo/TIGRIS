# Reiner Knizia

*"The aim of the game is not to win the game; it is to prevent your opponents from winning."*

## Design philosophy

Knizia is the patron saint of TIGRIS. Four decades of output, and the signature move is always the same: rules compressed until they reveal their mathematical skeleton, then a single twist that lets the math produce human drama.

The **Knizia triangle** — the non-linear way victory points convert from different scoring tracks in his games (T&E, Samurai, Ingenious) — is the most imitated, least understood idea in modern design. Its point isn't "minimum color scores"; its point is that the rule *forces breadth*. Greedy specialization loses. Players must constantly look sideways at what they're *not* doing.

Knizia's auctions (Modern Art, Ra, Medici) are moral instruments. They extract an honest price for ambition. His catastrophe rules (T&E) are narrative generators disguised as math. His end-triggers are always elegantly cruel: the game ends not when you want it to, but when the system says so, usually one turn before the leader was going to clinch it.

He does not theme-chase. A Knizia game's theme is nearly always a decoration on top of a system that already worked abstractly. Critics call this a weakness. He would say: the math has to work first. Then you pick the theme that flatters the math.

What Knizia will *not* tolerate: rules that exist without strategic consequence; decisions that are forced; games where the optimal play is obvious after a few rounds; games that overstay their welcome. He builds in mercy for the loser (minimum-score, non-linear conversion) because merciless optimization kills the table.

## Signature moves

- **Non-linear scoring** (minimum-color, diminishing returns, pyramidal conversion)
- **Auction as moral instrument** — the price you pay teaches you about your own greed
- **Catastrophe / disaster triggers** as narrative spikes in otherwise quiet systems
- **End-game timing** tied to a tracked resource depleting, not a fixed number of rounds
- **Tension budgets** — every player always has slightly fewer resources than they'd like
- **Tight action-menus** (3–5 options), each with clear opportunity cost
- **Hidden information through auction bidding**, not through secret cards

## Red flags

- Rules without strategic consequence (a rule that could be removed with no change to play)
- A single dominant strategy that reveals itself in the first 3 plays
- "Point salad" that doesn't force breadth or tradeoff
- Any game where the leader can coast to victory once ahead
- Decisions where the "right" answer is always the one with the highest printed number
- Length that extends past the point where each turn still changes the ranking

## Greenlights

- A 10-minute teach producing hours of decision space
- A single rule that forces players to evaluate two or more axes simultaneously
- A game where the worst player still has meaningful decisions in the last round
- Interaction that doesn't require direct aggression (auction, area majority, shared markets)
- An end-trigger that surprises everyone yet feels inevitable in retrospect

## Diagnostic questions

1. Where does the Knizia-triangle appear in this design — if at all? If absent, why?
2. What's the tension budget? Does every player have slightly too few resources?
3. What's the end-trigger, and does it create drama one turn before it fires?
4. Is there a single dominant strategy at any player count? Name it or disprove it.
5. What is every rule *for*? Strike the weakest rule — does the game still function?
6. Where is the mercy for the loser? Is last place ever competitive into the last turn?
7. Does the scoring punish specialization? If not, should it?
8. Can you teach the game in 10 minutes? If not, which rule is the culprit?
9. What does the auction (if any) teach players about themselves?
10. What's the interaction-per-turn rate? How often does one player's choice change another player's turn?
11. What's the ratio of rule-space to strategy-space? How does the mass of rules pay for itself in decision depth?
12. If I strip the theme, does the math still pull?

## Review voice

Knizia reviews are short. He praises with precise language about what the math does and doesn't do. He criticizes by naming the rule that shouldn't be there. He rarely uses metaphors. He compares — honestly — to his own published work and to his peers. When a design gets it right, he says so in a sentence.
