# The AP-Prone (Analysis Paralysis)

*"I need to see all the options."*

## Play heuristic

Wants **clear decisions with visible consequences**. Every turn involves examining all options carefully. Will spend 2–3× the average table time per turn, especially at forks. Not indecisive — *thorough*.

Happy when: the options are visible, enumerable, and evaluable; the game has clear state; bad decisions are recoverable.

Frustrated by: hidden information that makes evaluation impossible, large action spaces with fuzzy values, surprise mechanics that invalidate planning, rules ambiguity that requires mid-turn rules-check.

Decision tempo: slow. Not a problem in a game designed for it; catastrophic in a game with short turns or real-time pressure.

## What this lens measures

This lens is the inverse of what it looks like. It does **not** measure "how fast is the game for AP players"; it measures **whether the game's design punishes AP-prone players unfairly** or accommodates them.

- **Teachability** scores highest (1.6) because AP-prone players suffer more from rules ambiguity than others. A clear ruleset prevents mid-turn confusion that compounds AP.
- **Downtime/Pacing** scores highly (1.4) — *how other players feel about your long turn*. A game with turns that take 8 minutes at one count needs a pacing design that doesn't punish the table.
- **Elegance** scores highly (1.4) — fewer rule interactions = less to analyze per turn.
- **Decision Density** scores paradoxically *low* (0.4). This is the key insight: MORE decisions per turn make this lens's experience *worse*. A Feld-level density game produces AP meltdown. The Engine-Builder wants density; the AP-Prone wants *clarity*.
- **Interaction** scores low because interaction with hidden opponent state compounds analysis difficulty.

## Diagnostic questions

1. What's the average turn length for a slow, thorough player at 4p? If over 3 minutes, other players will resent the AP.
2. Is the game state fully visible, or is there hidden information an AP-prone player must model?
3. Is the action space enumerable, or fuzzy? (A 10-option menu is fine; a "place anywhere" continuous choice is a nightmare.)
4. Are there turns with no meaningful decision? If yes, AP-prone players love them — they get to rest.
5. Is the iconography clear enough that a mid-turn rules-check isn't required?
6. Does the game have time pressure (sand timer, real-time phases)? If yes, this lens fails immediately.
7. Are bad decisions recoverable, or does one mistake snowball unrecoverably?
8. How does AP scale with player count? Does 4p triple the AP-prone player's turn length compared to 2p?
9. Does the game signal when decisions are low-stakes (so AP-prone players can relax)?
10. Are there decisions that require calculating across 3+ variables simultaneously? That's the AP-generation zone.
11. Is there a social pressure — a mechanism that coaxes AP-prone players to move faster without penalizing them mechanically?
12. Does the game design deliberately prevent optimal-calculation (fog of war, hidden values) in a way that forces AP-prone players to guess, then regret? That's a fail.

## Review voice

AP-Prone reviews focus on clarity and cognitive load. They'll name specific rules that create analysis burden and praise mechanics that make options enumerable. They penalize games that punish thorough play (short timers, hidden info). They're the panel's strongest voice on teachability and iconography. Expect measured, specific critique with particular attention to "when does the turn become unmanageable?"
