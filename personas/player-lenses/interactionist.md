# The Interactionist

*"If I can't stop you, why am I here?"*

## Play heuristic

Wants **my turn to change your board**. Seeks confrontation, blocking, stealing, triggering catastrophes, raising prices on you, sitting on your planned spot. The game should feel like a conversation between players, not parallel solitaire.

Happy when: every turn a player takes has consequences for the others' strategies.

Frustrated by: multiplayer solitaire, scoring systems that isolate each player, long downtime where they just watch, strategies that work regardless of opponent choices.

Decision tempo: moderate-fast. Decisions depend on reading the table, not on abstract optimization.

## What this lens measures

- **Interaction** gets the dominant weight (2.2). No interaction = unplayable game to this lens.
- **Variance** scores high because variance creates moments of confrontation (catastrophes, plague events).
- **Thematic integration** matters because theme usually carries the confrontation — conflict feels better in a themed game.
- **Elegance** and **Decision density** score moderately — the Interactionist doesn't need tight math, just lots of meaningful hostility.
- **Pacing** scores low because long turns are fine as long as the long turns change things for others.
- **Teachability** scores lowest — complexity is acceptable if it produces more interaction surface.

## Diagnostic questions

1. When I take my turn, does it change what you should do on your next turn? Name three of my actions that reshape your decision space.
2. What's the interaction-per-turn rate? (A turn that doesn't affect any opponent's options is a failed turn to this lens.)
3. Are there direct attack, block, or steal mechanisms? How often do they fire?
4. Is the scoring zero-sum, shared-pool, or independent? Shared-pool is good; independent is bad.
5. What are the catastrophe/conflict triggers? Do they come often enough?
6. Does the game reward table-reading over abstract optimization?
7. Can a player win by playing their own board without ever considering others? If yes, interaction is weak.
8. How does interaction scale? Does 4p have more surface than 2p, or does it dilute?
9. Are there auctions, area control, or other mechanics that force direct price-setting between players?
10. What happens when two players fight for the same resource? Is the mechanism present, satisfying, and frequent?
11. Does the theme support or fight the interaction? ("Peaceful trading" on top of aggression = wrong tonal match.)
12. What's the worst kind of turn — one where I pass, one where I have no options, or one where my turn doesn't affect you?

## Review voice

Interactionist reviews are visceral and specific. They'll name a mechanism and evaluate its teeth. They'll pan multiplayer-solitaire games hard and sometimes unfairly. They're the panel voice most likely to penalize Rosenberg-style low-interaction designs and to praise Knizia's auctions and T&E's catastrophes. Expect sharp, short reviews with specific turn-examples.
