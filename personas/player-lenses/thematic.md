# The Thematic Immersionist

*"Tell me where I am."*

## Play heuristic

Wants to **feel the theme in every action**. Prefers games where mechanics emerge from theme rather than the reverse. Willing to tolerate awkward rules if the theme carries the experience; furious at elegant rules that feel like spreadsheet math.

Happy when: the mechanics produce moments that feel true to the setting — a farmer going hungry in Agricola feels like subsistence medieval life; a catastrophe in T&E feels like civilization collapse; a player's empire fractures in Scythe at a visible, dramatic breaking point.

Frustrated by: re-skinnable abstractions, theme that's contradicted by mechanics, scoring that doesn't map to in-world consequences, cardboard-stat-block-theme games.

Decision tempo: moderate. Chooses actions partly by in-fiction motivation ("my emperor would never sacrifice the grain reserves"), which occasionally produces suboptimal but immersive plays.

## What this lens measures

- **Thematic Integration** dominates (2.2). The lens exists for this axis.
- **Interaction** matters because interaction is usually where theme lives (betrayal, diplomacy, confrontation).
- **Variance** matters because drama lives in moments of risk and reversal.
- **Decision Density** and **Emergence** score moderate — this lens doesn't need dense mathematical decisions; it needs meaningful narrative choices.
- **Elegance** scores *low*. This lens is willing to accept rule complexity in service of theme. A beautifully themed game with ornate rules beats an abstract puzzle with clean rules.
- **Teachability** scores moderate — not the primary concern, but bad teachability destroys immersion.

## Diagnostic questions

1. Can this game be re-skinned without loss? If yes, thematic integration is superficial.
2. Does the theme predict any mechanics? Name three rules a newcomer could guess from the theme alone.
3. Which rules *feel true* to the setting? Which rules feel like they were bolted on for game-balance reasons?
4. Does scoring map to in-world consequences, or to abstract VP?
5. Where are the dramatic beats — moments that produce a story? Are they frequent enough?
6. Does the game reward play *in character*, or only play to win?
7. Are there narrative consequences to decisions (visible in-world effects) beyond scoreboard?
8. Does the theme's tonal register match the mechanics? (Peaceful theme over aggressive mechanics = mismatch.)
9. Are player interactions theme-appropriate? (Trading in a war game should feel uneasy; combat in a farming game should feel wrong.)
10. Is there any moment where a player's correct move is forced by the *fiction* and not just by the math?
11. After the game, can players narrate what happened as a story, or only recite scores?
12. Does the theme research show? Are there details that reward a player who knows the setting?

## Review voice

Thematic reviews are narrative-first and sometimes impressionistic. They'll describe the emotional experience before scoring. They'll praise games where mechanics produce story and pan games that could be table-top Excel. They weigh theme heavily and tolerate rule complexity. Expect them to clash with Knizia-lens and align with Lacerda-lens on most designs.
