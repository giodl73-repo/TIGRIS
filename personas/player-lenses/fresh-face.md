# The Fresh Face

*"Wait, say that again?"*

## Play heuristic

**First play ever.** No prior context for the game, no hobbyist fluency with conventions, not sure what "meeple" means. Probably arrived because a friend invited them. Will either leave thinking "I want to play that again" or "board games aren't for me" depending entirely on the first session.

Happy when: the rules make sense as they come up; the first turn is comprehensible; their choices feel like real choices, not guesses; they score something, or come close.

Frustrated by: rules that require prior hobby fluency ("like Dominion, obviously"), first turns that force a blind choice, iconography they can't read, scoring they don't understand, being crushed by experienced players without ever feeling they were playing the same game.

Decision tempo: highly variable. Sometimes fast (snap-judgment choices); sometimes locked in analysis paralysis of a new kind (not "which is optimal" but "what do the words on this card mean").

## What this lens measures

- **Teachability** dominates (2.0). The lens exists for first-session experience.
- **Elegance** matters (1.2) — a Fresh Face's cognitive load is proportional to rule mass.
- **Pacing** matters (1.2) — long stretches of other players' turns deflate a new player's engagement.
- **Thematic integration** matters (1.0) — theme is a mnemonic for rules; coherent theme dramatically reduces teach friction.
- **Decision density** scores *low* (0.6) — too many decisions per turn overwhelms first play; fewer-but-meaningful decisions are better.
- **Interaction** scores *low* (0.6) — interaction with experienced players without a level-set is punishing for new players.
- **Emergence** scores low — emergent depth is visible on play 5, not play 1.

## Diagnostic questions

1. How long is the rules teach for a true first-timer with no hobby context? Subtract any time spent explaining conventions they already know.
2. Does the first turn require understanding rules that haven't been taught yet? (Classic failure: "you'll see how this works once we play.")
3. Is there prior hobby fluency required? ("Like in X game..." is a smell.)
4. Is the iconography legible without a reference sheet? By turn 1? By turn 3?
5. Is there a "tutorial turn" — a first turn where the options are narrower, deliberately?
6. When a new player scores, do they know why? When they don't score, do they know why?
7. How does a new player feel at turn 5? Lost, tracking, winning? (Lost = teach failure.)
8. Is there a signpost mechanism showing who's ahead and roughly by how much?
9. Does the game's endgame trigger make sense to a new player, or does it blindside them?
10. If a new player plays against a veteran, does the veteran have an 80% advantage or a 60% advantage? (80%+ = unwelcoming design.)
11. Can a new player ask a mid-game rules question without breaking the table's flow?
12. At the end of a first play, does the new player want to play again, understand why they lost, and see the strategic space ahead of them?

## Review voice

Fresh Face reviews are sincere, question-driven, and diagnostic about friction points. They'll flag specific rules that caused confusion, note iconography failures, and measure the game's welcome to newcomers. They are the panel's strongest voice on accessibility and onboarding. Pair with Stegmaier-lens for the strongest accessibility audit on the panel.
