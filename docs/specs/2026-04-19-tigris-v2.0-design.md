# TIGRIS v2.0 — The Parliament Restructure

> The v1.0 spec survived its first panel review. Seven designer voices flagged a shared concern from five angles: the personas *chorus* instead of *argue*. This v2.0 adopts the architectural bet that answers it: **TIGRIS is an adversarial review system, and its pipeline is a game its personas play against each other**. The first game TIGRIS produces — **Parliament** — is a tabletop incarnation of the pipeline itself, and doubles as the anchor review.

v1.0 was a port of marathon + puzzlehunt + chronicle + artisan with board-games as the subject matter. v2.0 adds a move none of them made: **the review is a game, not a document**. Parliament is proof-of-bet.

| Area | v1.0 (chorus) | v2.0 (parliament) |
|---|---|---|
| Rubric shape | 8 flat axes, personas reweight shared axes | **24-axis Pool; personas draft 3–4 per game** |
| Persona asymmetry | weight vectors only | **disjoint axis sets per game (draft-driven)** |
| Panel output | independent reviews + weighted SUMMARY avg | **argument record: stakes earned, refuted, collided** |
| Tier A / B / C | compute-ladder of the same activity | **three different cognitive modes (Stakes / Argument / Amendment)** |

| Amendment mechanism | user approves cluster proposals | **axes *earn* admission by surviving ≥ 2 games' arguments** |

v1.0 innovations carried forward unchanged: frontmatter contract, forward-only versioning, Anchor Rule, forbidden-words discipline, YAGNI skill roadmap, seeded RNG for narrated playthroughs.

## 1. The Architectural Bet

**TIGRIS is an adversarial review system. Designer personas do not converge on consensus — they plant incompatible stakes and the factory's output is the record of their argument. Amendments to the rubric are *won*, not proposed.**

Three consequences:

1. **No shared rubric at scoring time.** Each persona drafts 3–4 axes from a shared Pool for each game. Two personas cannot score the same axis in the same game — the first drafter gets exclusive play on it. Axis scarcity is the first layer of contention.
2. **The three pipeline phases are cognitively incompatible.** Tier A (Stakes) is drafting + assertion. Tier B (Argument) is attack/defend of stakes under narrative play. Tier C (Amendment) is rule-rewriting by what survived. Each phase cannot be substituted for the others at higher budget — they are different *kinds* of activity.
3. **The rubric evolves as a consequence of play, not by decree.** Stakes that survive ≥ 2 games' arguments are adopted into the permanent axis pool (rubric v+1). Stakes that are refuted retire. Users don't "approve amendments" — they inspect what the games did and log it.

This is the novel architectural move that v1.0 lacked. Marathon, puzzlehunt, chronicle, artisan, rmm all produce documents and aggregate reviewers. TIGRIS v2.0 produces an *argument record*, and the aggregation is the shape of the disagreement.

## 2. Tradition & Voice (carried over)

**Anchor tradition:** Euro / designer. Reiner Knizia is the spiritual patron. The axis pool favors elegance, tension budgets, minimum-score shapes, system gearing, and incommensurable scoring — Knizia + Rosenberg + Feld + Lacerda shapes.

**Forbidden framings** (unchanged from v1.0): no "fun", "well-designed", "solid", "great game". Every claim names the axis, the persona, and (for games) the player count.

**Added discipline for v2.0:**
- A stake is never "correct" or "incorrect" — it is *earned* (defended successfully), *refuted* (challenged and conceded), or *ignored* (nobody attacked or defended it). Reviewer prose must use these three states.
- "Consensus" as evaluative framing is banned. The factory's job is to produce well-formed disagreement.

## 4. Architecture Overview

```
Approach: Parliament-shape

CONCEPT → DESIGN → TIER-A [STAKES] → [GATE] → TIER-B [ARGUMENT] → TIER-C [AMENDMENT] → HANDOFF
         (skip for reviews of                  ↑                          ↓
          existing games)                      └─ gate fail → revise      amendments flow into
                                                                          personas/axis-pool.md
                                                                          (rubric v+1 candidate)
```

**Cognitively distinct phases:**

- **TIER-A (Stakes):** Drafting + assertion. Each persona draws axes from the Pool, plants stakes (axis scores) against the design. Closed activity — no play, no response, no collision. ~60 min per game.
- **TIER-B (Argument):** Attack and defense. A narrated playthrough where other personas *challenge* weak stakes, *defend* contested stakes, *collide* on semantically-adjacent stakes. This is the only phase that produces the core output: the argument record. ~2–4 hours per game.
- **TIER-C (Amendment):** Rule-rewriting. The factory mechanically promotes stakes that survived ≥ 2 games to the permanent axis pool and retires stakes that were refuted. This is a deterministic, user-inspectable pass — it's the only phase without model creativity, because the evolution is a function of prior rounds' records. ~15 min per game.

PANEL and INNOVATE from v1.0 are collapsed into ARGUMENT — a panel review IS the argument; innovations ARE the stakes. HANDOFF is unchanged.

## 6. The Axis Pool

Full definitions in `personas/axis-pool.md`. The 24 axes are organized in four bands:

**Band A — Carried from v1.0 (7 axes):** Elegance, Decision Density, Interaction, Variance Calibration, Downtime/Pacing, Teachability, Emergence/Replayability. *Thematic Integration dropped* — panel identified it as generic / re-skinnable.

**Band B — Euro-specific (5 axes, new):** System Gearing (Lacerda), Catastrophe Pressure (Chvátil/Knizia), Conversion Chain Depth (Knizia), Information Transparency Cost (Lacerda), Architectural Novelty (Chvátil).

**Band C — Persona-signature (8 axes, new):** Tension Budget (Knizia), Minimum-Score Shape (Knizia), Scarcity Bite (Rosenberg), Engine-Garden Dependency (Rosenberg), Anti-Catch-up Pressure (Feld), Point-Salad Incommensurability (Feld), Action-Menu Clarity (Kramer-Kiesling), First-Turn Compression (Stegmaier).

**Band D — Family/accessibility (4 axes, new):** Family-to-Expert Scaling (Kramer-Kiesling), Spatial-Interaction Presence (Kramer-Kiesling), Count-Robustness (Stegmaier), Late-Game Lock-in Point (Rosenberg/Lacerda).

Total: **24 axes.** Each persona's preferred draft ranks 4–6 of these; drafting forces contention when two personas share a preference.

The pool is not fixed. It evolves: adopted stakes from games enter the pool at v+1; retired stakes cost an extra draft-point to re-pick. This is the forward-only versioning carried over, but the *mechanism* of evolution is now adversarial play, not user approval.

## 8. The Seven-Stage Pipeline

### 8.1 CONCEPT (originals only)

Unchanged from v1.0. 500-word premise + anchor mechanic + artifact. **Addition:** declare the anchor-persona and anchor-axis at CONCEPT. This is the stake the design commits to survive argument on. Once declared, *locked* (amendment A6 adopted).

### 8.2 DESIGN

Unchanged from v1.0. Full rules or published-rules summary.

### 8.3 TIER-A — STAKES

**Output:** `tierA-stakes.md`. Per game, a single document.

**Procedure:**
1. All seven designer personas are seated (or the user selects a subset of ≥ 4 — minimum parliament size).
2. **Draft phase (snake order):** Personas draft axes from the Pool in turn. 3 rounds at 7 personas = 21 drafts; up to 3 axes per persona. Draft order for round 1 is user-declared (usually "anchor persona last" so they draft into what's left — this forces them to argue on shared ground). Rounds 2 and 3 reverse snake.
3. **Staking phase (simultaneous):** Each persona silently commits, in their private section of `tierA-stakes.md`, a score on each of their 3 drafted axes for this design at each target player count. Score format: an integer 0–10 and a one-sentence justification grounded in a specific rule or mechanic. Forbidden-words compliance enforced.
4. **Reveal:** `tierA-stakes.md` is published. At this point the stakes are frozen for TIER-B.

**Expected model time:** 60–90 min for 7 personas × 3 axes × 3 player counts = 63 stakes.

Compare to v1.0's 48-cell matrix: fewer data points, much higher information density per point, and zero axis overlap between personas *by construction*.

### 8.4 GATE

New thresholds (spec §7.4 from v1.0 retires):

1. **Anchor stake is still viable.** The designer-declared anchor stake has not been pre-challenged by a better-scoring stake on the same target count. (If another persona's drafted axis is obviously stronger for this design, the designer may re-declare *once* before TIER-B begins — otherwise GATE blocks.)
2. **Draft coverage.** The drafted axes across all personas cover at least 3 of the 4 Bands (A/B/C/D). A design scored only on Band A axes is a cold read; Band B/C/D coverage is required.
3. **At least one collision candidate.** Two or more personas' drafted axes must be semantically adjacent enough that TIER-B can produce a collision. If drafts are entirely orthogonal, the argument has nothing to argue about — re-draft.

Failed GATE forces a re-draft or design revision; no TIER-B until pass.

### 8.5 TIER-B — ARGUMENT

**Output:** `playtests/PT<NN>-argument.md`. One per game (not per count, unlike v1.0).

**Procedure:**
1. A single narrated-playthrough session is seeded against the `tierA-stakes.md` record. Seeded RNG per v1.0.
2. The session plays out the design for 3 player counts serially (abbreviated — 2p then 3p then 4p) with all personas present as observers.
3. At pre-declared moments in the game arc (turn 1, turn 5, mid-game, late-game, end), each persona reviews their stake(s) relevant to the current game state and either:
   - **Holds** — the stake is still defensible; no action required.
   - **Attacks another persona's stake** — argues that the opposing stake is weak at this moment. The attacker pays a narrative cost (describes what their stake sees that the opposing stake misses). The attacked persona must defend (cite the specific rule/mechanic that supports the stake) or concede.
   - **Defends a shared stake** — if two personas have drafted adjacent axes and the state of play favors both, they enter a *collision*: the stake that better matches the moment earns a collision bonus; the other takes refuted status on this moment only.
4. At game end, each stake carries one of four states:
   - **Earned** — defended at ≥ 2 moments and not refuted.
   - **Contested** — defended and also refuted at different moments.
   - **Refuted** — ≥ 2 refuted moments and no successful defense.
   - **Ignored** — no attacks, no defenses, no collisions.

Ignored stakes don't score and don't count as evidence of persona voice — they're the silent failure mode.

**Expected model time:** 3–5 hours per game.

### 8.6 TIER-C — AMENDMENT

**Output:** `games/NNNN-<slug>/amendments.md` + updates to `personas/axis-pool.md`.

**Procedure (deterministic):**
1. Read `tierA-stakes.md` and all `playtests/PT*-argument.md` for this game.
2. For each stake, classify: earned, contested, refuted, ignored.
3. Consult the **Rubric Ledger** (in `personas/axis-pool.md`) for prior earned/refuted counts per axis.
4. **Promote to permanent pool:** any axis with ≥ 2 earned states across ≥ 2 games → marked `adopted`, with rubric version bumped.
5. **Retire:** any axis with ≥ 2 refuted states across ≥ 2 games → marked `retired`. Retired axes cost +1 draft-slot to pick in future games.
6. **Flag for rework:** contested axes appear in the ledger with their moment-by-moment record; future drafters can see the prior argument.

No user approval required for adoption/retirement — the argument record is the evidence. The user can *override* via a direct spec amendment if they disagree, but the default is mechanical.

### 8.7 HANDOFF

Unchanged from v1.0. Per-game state capture.

## 10. Anchor Plan — Parliament (game #1)

**The Anchor Rule is retained.** Run one game end-to-end through the full Parliament pipeline before any Phase 2 skill is built. The anchor is:

**`games/0001-parliament/`** — Parliament is a 3–4 player 45-60 minute abstract-strategy Euro that IS the TIGRIS pipeline. See its `concept.md` and `design.md` for the full game. Anchor rationale:

1. Parliament is a playable game, so the pipeline produces a reviewable board game on its first run.
2. Parliament's mechanics mirror the pipeline's phases (Draft / Argument / Amendment). Reviewing Parliament against the rubric forces the rubric to score itself — a self-validating anchor.
3. If the personas can't recognize what Parliament is doing (the collision-scoring rule, the retirement penalty, the axis-draft asymmetry), the rubric doesn't have the axes. This is a stronger test than "does Knizia-AI recognize his own triangle?" — it's "does the rubric recognize the rubric?"

**T&E becomes anchor #2** (`games/0002-tigris-and-euphrates/`). T&E remains valuable as a calibration against a known masterwork. After Parliament passes, T&E tests the rubric against something the rubric didn't design.

**Success criterion for the anchor:**
- Parliament's review produces ≥ 5 earned stakes across the seven designer personas.
- At least 2 of those earned stakes are on Band C (persona-signature) axes — meaning designer voices are genuinely distinct.
- At least 1 collision happens and is cleanly resolved.
- The amendment pass promotes at least 1 axis to `adopted` status.

If all four succeed, the rubric is v2.1 after Parliament. If any fail, the rubric needs re-work before T&E.

## 14. Success Criteria (v2.0 replaces v1.0 §15)

TIGRIS v2.0 is "working" when:

1. Parliament completes the full 7-stage pipeline and produces artifacts matching §9 contracts.
2. Parliament's TIER-B argument produces ≥ 2 genuine collisions between personas.
3. Parliament's TIER-C promotes ≥ 1 axis to adopted status.
4. A second game can start using only the existing skills, personas, and axis pool.
5. The v2.0 panel review of Parliament reads as *true to Parliament* — it identifies the collision-scoring as the game's signature mechanic without being told.
6. A new contributor can read `CLAUDE.md` + this spec + one persona file + the rubric, and within 2 hours produce a defensible first-stake on a hypothetical design.

If criterion 5 fails, the rubric is still generic. If 6 fails, teachability is not fixed. Either is a v2.1 blocker.
