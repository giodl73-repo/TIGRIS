# v2.20 — Orthogonal-Preservation Collision Formalization

## Problem

Under the current collision rule (`personas/playtest-rubric.md` §Collide action, v2.19), a collision between two semantically-adjacent stakes resolves by comparing which stake better matches the current moment — **one earns, one takes a refutation**. This is the canonical collision outcome and is appropriate when two personas genuinely dispute which axis better characterizes the same phenomenon.

But across 21 games, **5 collisions resolved differently**: the panel voted that both stakes address genuinely orthogonal dimensions of the same moment, and neither invalidated the other. The collision was real (the axes fired together, indicating adjacency), but the resolution was **mutual preservation** rather than winner-takes-all.

The 5 instances:

| # | Game | Axes | Description | Vote |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | AFoS #16 | A7↔C4 | Emergence-Replayability vs Engine-Garden Dependency — Halifax Hammer's determinism challenged A7 while its engine build challenged C4; both charges were true simultaneously | Majority |
| 2 | Wingspan #17 | A3↔C4 | Interaction vs Engine-Garden — multiplayer-solitaire critique targeted A3 while the solo-engine observation targeted C4; neither subsumed the other | Majority |
| 3 | Wingspan #17 | A6↔C6 | Teachability vs Point-Salad Incommensurability — complexity-of-teach and complexity-of-scoring are distinct phenomena; the same mechanism (cards-as-habitat) contributed to both | Majority |
| 4 | Nemesis #20 | B5↔A7 | Architectural Novelty vs Emergence-Replayability — the alien lifecycle's structural novelty (B5) and the mission-branching's replability (A7) address different analytical registers; **unanimous 8-0** | 8-0 unanimous |
| 5 | Rewild #21 | C3↔C4 | Scarcity Bite vs Engine-Garden Dependency — the Wildlife deck scarcity constrained resource access (C3) while the card-combo construction created engine dependency (C4); both simultaneously operative | Majority |

**The current rule has no name or ledger treatment for this outcome.** When an OP-collision occurs, the session record notes it informally and both axes receive defense marks, but the protocol does not specify:
- What vote threshold triggers the outcome vs. a standard winner-takes-all resolution
- How the defend marks are recorded in the ledger
- Whether OP-collisions count toward the "≥ 1 clean collision resolution" success criterion
- What prevents spurious OP-votes (personas avoiding difficult arguments by declaring orthogonality)

Without formalization, each instance is ad-hoc. Cluster of 5 instances creates ambiguity about what the collision action means.

## Proposed amendment (v2.20 A-v2.20-01)

Add **Orthogonal-Preservation (OP)** as a named, first-class collision outcome alongside the existing winner-takes-all outcome (hereafter **Contested-Resolution**).

### Definitions

**Contested-Resolution (CR)** — existing default. Two stakes on adjacent axes, one better characterizing the moment. One persona earns collision credit (+1 defend); the other takes single-moment refutation (+1 refute). Whichever stake's axis more specifically explains the observed behavior wins.

**Orthogonal-Preservation (OP)** — new outcome. Two stakes on adjacent axes address genuinely different analytical registers of the same moment: both observations are simultaneously true and neither subsumes the other. Both personas earn a defend credit (+1 defend each); neither takes a refutation.

### OP vote threshold

- **Standard OP**: majority vote of present designers (≥ 5 of 8, or ≥ majority of designers present in the session).
- **Decisive OP**: unanimous or near-unanimous (≥ 7 of 8). Recorded as "unanimous OP" in the argument file; counts double toward the "collision resolution" success criterion (see §Success criterion interaction for floor constraint).
- **Failed OP motion**: if a persona proposes OP but the panel votes CR (< majority for OP), the collision proceeds as Contested-Resolution normally.

The vote must be **motivated**: the persona proposing OP must name the orthogonal registers (e.g., "B5 addresses structural architecture; A7 addresses long-run replayability — these are analytically independent"). Three register types have been confirmed by the corpus (v1.2 editorial addition, I-canton-02):

- **Structural register**: one axis addresses the mechanism's architecture; the other addresses the experience it produces (e.g., B5 Architectural Novelty ↔ A7 Emergence at Nemesis #20).
- **Social register**: one axis addresses player-to-player dynamics; the other addresses the game's internal mechanism (e.g., A3 Interaction ↔ C4 Engine-Garden at Wingspan #17).
- **Temporal register**: the same mechanism operates at two different time-scales — within a single play vs. across multiple plays (e.g., A6 Teachability ↔ C8 First-Turn Compression at Pandemic Legacy #22; C6 Point-Salad ↔ A7 Emergence at GARNER #23; D2 Spatial-Interaction ↔ A7 Emergence at CANTON #24). Three confirmed instances make temporal-register OP the most reliably produced OP type in the corpus. A bare "both are valid" is not sufficient and must be ruled CR by default. The panel collectively adjudicates whether a rationale names orthogonal registers; the proposing persona cannot self-certify. If the panel disagrees on adequacy, a simple majority vote resolves: majority for adequate → OP vote proceeds; majority for inadequate → collision resolves immediately as CR.

### Ledger treatment

| Outcome | Axis-1 ledger | Axis-2 ledger |
|---|---|---|
| CR (winner takes all) | +1 defend (winner) | +1 refute (loser) |
| OP (both preserved) | +1 defend | +1 defend |

OP-collision defend marks count identically to standard defend marks toward adoption thresholds (≥ 2 earned across ≥ 2 games). There is no discount for being earned via OP rather than CR.

### Argument file notation

In `PT<NN>-argument.md`, OP-collisions are marked:

```
COLLISION (OP): <Persona-A> <Axis-X> ↔ <Persona-B> <Axis-Y>
  Proposing OP: <Persona-A>
  Rationale: <one sentence naming orthogonal registers>
  Vote: <N>-<M> for OP  [or "unanimous 8-0"]
  Outcome: both axes earn defend marks.
```

CR-collisions continue with existing notation; no change required.

### Success criterion interaction

The existing success criterion "≥ 1 clean collision resolution" (personas/playtest-rubric.md §Success criteria) is satisfied by either CR or OP outcomes. A Decisive OP (≥ 7-0) counts as **2** collision resolutions toward this criterion, rewarding high-confidence orthogonality recognition.

**Criterion §3 floor (A-spec-2.20-01)**: Decisive OP double-credit does not substitute for a standard collision. A game that produces only Decisive OP events and no CR or standard-OP collisions does not satisfy criterion §3. At least one CR or standard-OP collision must occur independently of any Decisive OP. The double-credit supplements this baseline; it does not replace it.

### Rate-limiting

v2.20 is a protocol-addition amendment. It consumes the standard 2-game cycle amendment rate-limit. Games #21 (Rewild) and #20 (Nemesis) are the evidence cycle; v2.20 adopts as their follow-on protocol amendment.

## Worked examples

### Example 1 — B5↔A7 (Nemesis #20, unanimous 8-0)

**Collision setup**: Stegmaier staked B5 Architectural Novelty on Nemesis's alien-lifecycle mechanism. Lacerda staked A7 Emergence-Replayability on the branching mission structure.

At the late-game anchor, both axes fired: the alien lifecycle demonstrated structural novelty (no other game uses kill-rate-as-timer in this way) while the mission catalog demonstrated emergence (distinct playthroughs). A Lacerda proposed OP: "B5 asks 'is the architecture unprecedented?' and A7 asks 'does play state diverge across sessions?' These are different analytical questions — both are currently yes."

Panel voted 8-0. Both axes earned defend marks. Neither took a refutation. This became the strongest OP-collision in the corpus.

**Protocol implication**: when an axis addresses *structural uniqueness* and another addresses *experiential variance*, these are paradigmatically orthogonal registers. OP is the correct resolution.

### Example 2 — A3↔C4 (Wingspan #17, majority vote)

**Collision setup**: Chvátil staked A3 Interaction on Wingspan's multiplayer-solitaire concern. Rosenberg staked C4 Engine-Garden Dependency on Wingspan's tableau-as-engine architecture.

At mid-game: Chvátil attacks C4 ("the engine garden is so self-sufficient it eliminates the interaction A3 depends on"). Rosenberg defends C4 ("the engine garden is the mechanism itself; A3 critiques how interaction is structured, C4 critiques how the engine is structured — different questions about the same tableau").

Panel voted 5-3 for OP. Both axes earned defend marks.

**Protocol implication**: when one axis addresses a game's internal mechanism architecture and another addresses its multi-player-social dynamics, OP is often appropriate. The "mechanism vs. social" register distinction is a recurring pattern.

### Example 3 — A6↔C6 (Wingspan #17, majority vote)

**Collision setup**: Stegmaier staked A6 Teachability on first-play complexity. Feld staked C6 Point-Salad Incommensurability on end-scoring comparison difficulty.

At late-game: both axes fired simultaneously — the card-as-habitat mechanism adds both teaching complexity (A6) and scoring-pathway incommensurability (C6).

**Protocol implication**: when the same mechanism produces distinct complexity burdens at distinct phases of the game (learn-to-play vs. score-comparison), OP is appropriate. A6 and C6 measure different temporal moments of complexity.

## Non-goals

- **OP as a way to avoid losing**: a collision must be genuinely motivated. If a panel member proposes OP to avoid losing a collision vote, the motivated-rationale requirement blocks this. Unmotivated OP motions default to CR.
- **OP between non-adjacent axes**: OP only applies to collisions (axes that fire simultaneously on the same moment). Non-adjacent axes cannot produce an OP outcome — they simply don't collide.
- **Retroactive reclassification of prior collisions**: the 5 historical instances are already recorded and their defend/refute marks are already in the ledger. v2.20 formalizes the name and rules going forward. No retroactive ledger changes.
- **OP as a special adoption pathway**: OP defend marks are ordinary defend marks. There is no "OP-bonus" to adoption thresholds.

## Open questions (resolved)

1. **Does a failed OP motion cost anything?** No — failing to achieve OP majority simply means the collision resolves as CR. No additional penalty to the proposing persona. *Decided: zero cost for failed OP motion; keeps proposals clean and unstrategic.*

2. **Can both personas propose OP simultaneously?** Yes — either or both personas at a collision may propose OP; the panel then votes once. If both propose, the motivated-rationale is drawn from both proposals. *Decided: joint proposal is fine; single vote.*

3. **Does OP count toward the amendment rate limit?** Yes — this is a first-class protocol addition (not a ledger-movement mechanism like v2.18). It consumes the current cycle's rate-limit slot. *Decided: yes, rate-limited.*

4. **What is the minimum meaningful OP corpus?** 5 instances is sufficient to distinguish the pattern from noise. Each of the 5 shows a different axis-pair. The Nemesis 8-0 unanimous vote establishes canonical OP. *Decided: proceed at 5 instances.*

5. **Should OP-collisions count toward the "≥ 1 clean collision resolution" success criterion?** Yes, identically to CR. Decisive OP (≥ 7-0) counts as 2 toward the criterion. *Decided: yes, and decisive OP gets double credit.*
