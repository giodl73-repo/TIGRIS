# Brass: Birmingham — Rules Summary

## 1. Overview
2–4 players, 60–120 minutes. Two-era economic engine game set during the British Industrial Revolution. Players build industries, develop rail/canal links, and consume resources to score VP. Canal Era is played first; after scoring, all networks are cleared, and the Rail Era is played on the same map with stricter connection rules. Highest total VP across both eras wins.

## 2. Key Mechanics
- **Industrial cascade (B1)**: Coal → Iron → Rails/Ships. Building a rail link requires coal. Coal mines require iron. Iron requires coal to build. The circular dependency resolves via shared resources: players may use opponents' resources (paying the owner) to build their own industries.
- **Card-driven actions**: Each turn, play one card from hand and take two actions. Cards specify which cities you may build in. Deck management is critical — spent cards are discarded and not available for the second era.
- **Two-era structure (D4/C6)**: Canal Era: canals connect cities; industries may share canals freely. Rail Era: rails require coal to build; you can only build in cities connected to your existing network OR to an opponent's rail. Canals score VP at end of Canal Era but are then removed. Rail-era industries and rails score at end of Rail Era. The scoring formulas diverge: canal-era factories score by level; rail-era merchants score by adjacent trade house connections. These registers are incommensurable.
- **Overdevelopment and collapse (B2)**: If a resource type is depleted from a region, players blocking the supply chain create systemic collapse pressure.
- **Resource scarcity (C3)**: Coal and iron are genuinely scarce. The market (central supply) provides resources but at increasing cost as demand rises.

## 3. Era Transition (D4 permanent lock-in)
At the end of the Canal Era, all canal tiles are removed from the board — including the links you built. Only industries (factories, mines, ports) survive into the Rail Era. This is permanent and irreversible: canal-era network investment cannot be preserved, only its scoring captured before the transition.
