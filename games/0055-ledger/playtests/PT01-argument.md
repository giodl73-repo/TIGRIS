## Round 1 — Calibration bids

**C1 (Knizia anchor) — Defend**: "Round 1: Lot = [Grain-7, Grain-4, Metal-2] ≈ 13 VP value. P2 must bid. What does P1 bid? What does P3 bid? P2 has no information. The only signal is the lot value (13 VP estimated) and the fact that rival tokens run 1–10. P2 bids 6 — enough to win if others are conservative, not catastrophic if others bid aggressively. Reveals: P1=5, P2=6, P3=8, P4=4. P3 wins; pays P2's bid of 6 (second highest). P2's 6 is refunded but P3 permanently spends 6 tokens. The tension was live before the reveal: P2 didn't know if 6 was enough. C1 earns from Round 1. +1 defend."

**B4 (Lacerda) — Defend**: "Round 1: P4 watches P3 win with bid 8 paying 6. P4 now knows P3's maximum potential bid was ≥ 8. But for Round 2, P3 has spent 6 tokens permanently — P3's remaining bid capacity is reduced. P4 recalibrates: P3 is now weaker. The information from Round 1 bids persists into Round 2 as evidence about rivals' remaining budget. Sealed bids create an information economy that compounds across rounds. B4 earns. +1 defend."

**A2 (Vaccarino) — Defend**: "Round 1: P1 must calculate (a) lot value (~13 VP), (b) likely threshold (unknown; estimate from prior game knowledge), (c) budget implication (paying 5 tokens is significant vs. total 10-token pool). P1 spends 45 seconds calculating before bidding 5. Per-round decision density is high: three variables simultaneously under sealed-bid uncertainty. A2 earns. +1 defend."

## Round 3 — Compressed budgets

**C1 (Knizia) — 2nd Defend**: "Round 3: P3 has spent 6 tokens total across Rounds 1–2. P3 has only 4 tokens remaining in their payment capacity. P3 faces Round 3 lot worth ~11 VP and knows they can bid at most 4. P3 calculates: bid 4 (might win on a weak round) vs. bid 3 (save 1 token for Round 4 optionality). Budget is demonstrably compressed. The tension has escalated across rounds. C1's structural scarcity is multi-round compounding. +1 defend."

### C1↔B4 COLLISION — Round 3 (OP, temporal-register)

**Knizia (C1 anchor) proposes OP**: "C1 fires on the moment of bid reveal — the tension of not knowing whether your bid was enough. C1 is the temporal experience of perpetual uncertainty: when you place your bid, you don't know the threshold. B4 fires on the information economy of sealed bids across rounds — the accumulating knowledge of rivals' remaining budget from prior bid reveals. C1 = the tension AT the moment of bidding; B4 = the information architecture BETWEEN rounds. Different temporal registers."

**Lacerda (B4 stake) seconds OP**: "C1 describes the experience of the Vickrey mechanism (the tension of the unknown threshold). B4 describes the cross-round information economy (learning rivals' budgets from prior reveals). C1 is about what makes each bid tense; B4 is about what you learn from each revealed bid for the next round. I can have C1 in a one-round auction (no B4 cross-round learning). I can have B4 information accumulation without C1 tension (if I knew the threshold). They decouple."

**Panel votes OP (6-2):**
- Rosenberg → OP: "A4 variance fires on tile distribution; C1 fires on bid-moment tension; B4 fires on cross-round learning. Three distinct questions."
- Chvátil → OP: "A3 interaction and B4 information economy are both consequences of sealed-bidding, but they're not the same as C1's tension experience."
- K-K → OP: "C7 clarity and A5 simultaneous pacing are orthogonal to C1 and B4. Similarly C1 and B4 address different temporal moments."
- Stegmaier → OP: "D3 scaling doesn't help resolve C1 vs. B4. They're orthogonal to each other."
- Feld → CR (C1 wins): "B4 is just C1's mechanism described from the information angle. You can't separate the tension from the hidden-bid structure. CR: C1."
- Vaccarino → CR (C1 wins): "B4's information economy is what creates C1's tension. They're the same mechanism described from two sides."

**Result: 6-2 OP** (Knizia + Lacerda + Rosenberg + Chvátil + K-K + Stegmaier = 6 OP; Feld + Vaccarino = 2 CR). C1 and B4 both earn. **28th temporal-register OP.**
