# RITE — Concept

## Premise

An initiation ritual is underway. Nobody speaks of what stage they have reached.

Three to six players enter the ceremony as Novices and must advance through four hidden
stages — Novice, Apprentice, Initiate, Elder — by performing the correct ritual acts in
the correct ritual zones. The room is the ceremony. The ceremony is the deduction.

Your current stage determines what ritual acts you are permitted to perform. It also
determines what triggers your Reaction — you may only respond to acts performed by
someone exactly one stage ahead of you. This means your reactions are information: anyone
watching closely can infer your stage from which acts you respond to. Advance your rite
without letting the others read your progression sequence.

The tension lives in the implicit contract every player accepts on sitting down: do not
simply announce your stage. Do not point at someone's reaction and say "they're an
Apprentice." Read the room. Let the ceremony do its work. This contract — unwritten,
unenforceable, essential — is what makes RITE a game rather than a puzzle.

Players move between five ritual zones in the room. Each zone carries a different
ceremonial weight and permits different acts. Performing the right act in the right zone
at the right stage advances you. Performing the wrong act wastes your turn and, worse,
signals something true about where you are. Completing all four stages before the
ceremony ends — before any other player maps your progression — wins.

RITE asks something unusual of euro players: stand up. Move. Perform. But it earns that
ask by wrapping the physical in the ceremonial. You are not doing charades. You are
completing a rite. That is a different social contract — one that serious players accept.

## Players and length

- **Players:** 3–6
- **Length:** 30–45 min
- **Weight:** 1.5 (BGG-estimate)

## Anchor mechanic

The reaction chain. Your Reaction card sits face-up and fires when a specific ritual act
is performed by a player exactly one stage ahead of you. When it fires, you must respond
physically — move to them, mirror their act, complete the response gesture. You cannot
choose NOT to respond when your trigger fires: the reaction is involuntary by design.
This involuntariness is the game's core: it means you are always leaking information
about your stage, and the skill is in managing what you leak — performing ambiguously
enough that your reaction is hard to read, while reading others' reactions clearly enough
to map their stages. The anchor mechanic earns A26 (social contract: you must respond,
and the group implicitly agrees to read rather than announce) and A27 (physical
affordance: the reaction is a bodily act, non-digitizable, load-bearing for the scoring).

## Artifact-as-story

**The Ritual Zone map.** Five physical positions in the room — marked by cards placed
on the floor or on surfaces — constitute the ceremony's sacred geography. The map is
different every game (zones are placed by the eldest player before the ceremony begins,
using five zone cards drawn from a shuffled deck of twelve). Which zones are adjacent,
which are central, which are liminal, changes the ritual structure entirely. The zone map
is the game's board — it just happens to be the room itself.

Secondary artifact: **the Stage card** (held hidden in hand). One card per player,
showing their current stage. Players advance by flipping their stage card privately when
they successfully complete a stage's progression requirement. No public announcement.
The flip is the only moment of private relief in an otherwise fully-observed game.

## Inspiration / lineage

- **Vlaada Chvátil — Codenames (2015):** The social contract that makes a constrained
  communication game work; the implicit norm of "read the signal, don't ask for
  clarification." RITE extends this by making the constraint physical and involuntary.
- **Antoine Bauza — Hanabi (2010):** Information inversion — you manage a state you
  cannot directly observe, inferring from others' behavior. RITE's stage is your hidden
  hand; others' reactions are your clues.
- **True American (New Girl, TV) / Johnny Whoops (folk game):** The room as the board;
  physical acts as the language; the implicit social contract that everyone pretends not
  to name what they are reading. RITE is the designed, non-drinking version of this
  social architecture.
- **Donald Vaccarino — Dominion (2008):** Reaction cards that fire involuntarily during
  others' turns. RITE's reaction chain is Dominion's Moat mechanic applied to bodies
  in space rather than cards in hand.

Novelty claim: **No corpus game (152 games reviewed) has made an involuntary physical
reaction the primary information-leakage mechanism.** Hanabi constrains communication.
The Mind constrains it further. RITE makes the leak physically embodied and unavoidable
— you cannot choose not to respond, and your response reveals you.

## Target review in the TIGRIS pipeline

**Predicted earned:**
- **A26 Social Contract Dependency (anchor):** The game requires — and cannot encode —
  the norm that players read rather than announce. Break the contract and the ceremony
  ends. A26 at maximum: RITE fails completely if players violate the implicit norm.
- **A27 Physical Affordance:** The reaction is a bodily act. A digital version loses
  the core — you cannot fake or mask a physical response the way you can a verbal one.
  Bodies in space are the scoring surface.
- **A25 Emotional Arc:** RITE has a designed narrative shape. You begin as a Novice and
  end (if you win) as an Elder who completed the ceremony before anyone mapped you.
  That arc is felt. Players recount their progression.
- **A32 Information Architecture Type:** RITE's information structure is inverted: you
  know your own stage but must infer others'. Others know their stages but must infer
  yours — from reactions you cannot suppress. This is closer to A32=8 (approaching
  Hanabi's inverted visibility structure) than to standard hidden-hand (A32=3).
- **A7 Emergence/Replayability:** Zone map changes every game (5 of 12 zones drawn).
  Stage progression sequences create different deduction problems. Role of Elder
  (map-setter) rotates. High variety from a small component set.
- **B5 Architectural Novelty:** Involuntary physical reaction as information-leakage
  mechanism has no corpus sibling.

**Predicted refuted/retire-explicit:**
- **D3 Count-Robustness:** Ceremony dynamics differ significantly at 3p vs 6p. At 3p,
  each player has fewer reactions to observe. At 6p, the room is dense with signals.
  Retire-explicit: the game is tuned best at 4–5p.
- **D1 Family-to-Expert:** Weight 1.5 but the physical ask and social contract require
  players who take the premise seriously. Not a family game. Retire-explicit.
- **B2 Catastrophe Pressure:** No catastrophe mechanism. RITE is slow-burn deduction,
  not reversal-event driven. Retire-explicit.

**Predicted OP collision:**
- **A26↔A27 OP** — social contract (the norm that makes the ceremony work) vs. physical
  affordance (the body-in-space reaction that generates the information). Both fire on
  the involuntary-reaction mechanism from different analytical registers.
- **B5↔A26 OP** — architectural novelty (involuntary physical leak is corpus-novel) vs.
  social contract (the norm that makes the leak meaningful rather than arbitrary).

## Non-goals

- Not a drinking game. The ceremony framing is the alternative to alcohol as social
  lubricant — it gives permission without requiring it.
- Not a deduction game in the Cluedo sense. You are not solving a murder. You are
  reading a living ceremony in progress.
- Not a performance game in the Charades sense. There is no audience and no performer.
  Everyone is simultaneously performing and reading.
- Not a hidden-role elimination game. Nobody is voted out. The game is advancement,
  not survival.
- Not a table game. RITE requires the room. Players must be able to physically move
  between five distinct positions. A dining table variant is possible but secondary.

## Open questions (to resolve during DESIGN)

1. **Reaction trigger specificity:** How specific must the trigger be? Too specific
   (exact gesture) = robotic and unnatural. Too broad (any act of a certain type) =
   everyone fires on everything. Design target: triggers are act-TYPE plus ZONE, not
   exact gesture — "an offering act in the Liminal zone" rather than "left hand raised."

2. **Stage advancement requirement:** What exactly must you do to advance a stage?
   Options: (a) complete 3 acts of your stage-type without triggering a wrong reaction,
   (b) perform a specific act in a specific zone while unobserved, (c) have your
   Reaction fire successfully N times in a row. Test (a) first.

3. **Zone map procedure:** Who places zones? Current: eldest player places all 5 zones
   using drawn zone cards before ceremony begins. Alternative: zones placed collaboratively
   in round-robin (more variance; zone placement itself is an information signal).

4. **Win condition:** First to complete all 4 stages? OR most stages completed when
   someone else's stage map is fully deduced (publicly called)? Current: first to
   complete 4 stages undetected. Test this; the "calling" mechanic may need to exist
   as a secondary win path.

5. **What happens when you're read?** If another player correctly publicly names your
   current stage: do you (a) lose a stage and regress, (b) freeze for one round, or
   (c) game ends and caller scores? Option (a) preserves game length; option (c) creates
   a sharp end condition. Test (a).

6. **The Elder role:** Does the stage-4 player (Elder) have special abilities, or are
   they simply one step from winning? Consider: Elders may watch but not perform — their
   final advancement requires a purely observational act rather than a physical one.
   This would make the Elder stage feel genuinely different from the previous three.

7. **Player count calibration:** At 3p the reaction chain is short (only 2 stages ahead
   to observe). At 6p the room is dense. Scale test: does the zone count need to increase
   with player count, or does the density itself change the game productively?
