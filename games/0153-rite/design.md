# RITE — Rules

## 1. Overview & Object of the Game

The ceremony has begun. You do not know who among you is further along than you are.
You are not permitted to say.

RITE is a 3–6 player social deduction and physical ritual game played in a room.
Players advance secretly through four ceremonial stages — Novice, Apprentice, Initiate,
Elder — by performing the correct ritual acts in the correct ritual zones. Every act
you perform is observed. Every reaction you make is involuntary and revealing. The room
is reading you while you read the room.

The player who first completes the Elder's final act in the Sanctum zone wins the
ceremony. But reaching the Elder stage means your reactions are the most frequent and
most visible — you are closest to winning and closest to being read. The ceremony's
architecture works against those who advance fastest.

Running beneath the main race is the **hierarchy**. The player at the highest stage
holds the Officiant role — temporary authority over the ceremony's flow. The Officiant
can disrupt any player's turn by entering their zone and commanding deference. But
the Officiant is also the most exposed: everyone watches the leader.

## 2. Components

**Zone cards — 12 cards, draw 5 per game**
Large format cards (A5) placed flat on surfaces or the floor to mark ritual zones.
Each shows the zone name, its act-type association, and its advancement function.

Five zone types used in a standard game:
- **Threshold** — zone of entry and departure; Novice advance zone
- **Liminal** — zone of in-between and waiting; Apprentice advance zone
- **Altar** — zone of offering and giving; Initiate advance zone
- **Sanctum** — zone of completion and arrival; Elder win zone
- **Passage** — zone of movement and transit; neutral (no advance function)

Seven additional zone variants (used in extended/variant play):
Chamber, Circle, Font, Hearth, Margin, Pyre, Vestibule. Each carries a unique
secondary rule described on the card face.

**Stage cards — 1 per player (5 total)**
Credit-card sized, held privately. Shows current stage on the face; back is identical
for all players (plain ritual pattern). Player privately flips or exchanges their
card as they advance (a small supply of Novice/Apprentice/Initiate/Elder cards is
kept facedown in a central pile — players swap quietly when advancing).

**Act Reference cards — 4 cards (one per act type)**
Optional. Summarizes gesture examples for each act type. Placed face-up on a shared
surface visible to all. Not required once players know the acts.

**Officiant token — 1**
A physical object passed to the highest-stage player. Must be visibly held or worn
(worn around wrist, held in hand, placed on shoulder). When you hold it, the room
knows you are currently the most senior — not your exact stage, but your relative
seniority.

**Challenge tokens — 3 per player at game start**
Used to make Calls (naming another player's stage). Each Call costs 1 Challenge token.
You cannot Call without a token. The Officiant earns +1 Challenge token at the start
of each new circuit while they hold the role.

**Score tokens — supply**
1 token per correct Call. Negative markers for wrong Calls (paperclip, different
colored token — any distinguisher).

## 3. Setup

1. **Clear the room.** Players need to move freely between five physical positions.
   Push furniture to create open paths between zones.

2. **Place zones.** The player holding the Officiant token at start (determined by
   draw or eldest) draws 5 Zone cards from the shuffled deck and places them as
   physical locations — on chairs, in corners, against walls, on the floor. Minimum
   2 meters between any two zones. The Sanctum must not be adjacent to the Threshold.

3. **Distribute Stage cards.** Each player takes a Novice Stage card and holds it
   privately. All additional stage cards (Apprentice, Initiate, Elder) are placed
   in a face-down pile at the center of the room — the **Stage Pile**.

4. **Distribute Challenge tokens.** Each player takes 3 Challenge tokens, held openly.

5. **Distribute Act Reference cards.** Place all 4 face-up where all players can see
   (a central table, a wall, etc.). These are public information.

6. **Pass the Officiant token** to the player who drew and placed the zones. They hold
   it for the first circuit.

7. **The Opening.** All players move to the Threshold zone. The Officiant performs one
   Completing act (see §5). This signals the ceremony's start. No reactions fire during
   the Opening — it is a ritual frame, not a scored turn.

## 4. Turn Structure

Play proceeds clockwise from the Officiant. Each player's turn has three phases:

### Phase 1 — The Officiant's Entry (if applicable)

If the Officiant is not in the same zone as the current player, and the Officiant
chooses to enter that player's zone this turn, the Officiant moves first and all
players already in that zone must immediately perform a **Deference act** (see §8).
The current player's turn is suspended until Deference is complete.

The Officiant may choose NOT to enter. This is always an option — entering is power
but also reveals the Officiant's strategic attention.

### Phase 2 — The Player's Turn

The active player takes their turn:
- **Move** to a zone (see §5, Action 1), then
- **Perform** an act (see §5, Action 2), OR
- **Call** another player's stage (see §5, Action 3), OR
- **Defer** — skip their performance and perform a Witnessing act toward any player
  (see §5, Action 4)

### Phase 3 — Reactions

After the active player's performance, check reactions:
- Any player whose current stage must react to the act type just performed must
  immediately stand and react (see §8 Reaction Chain).
- Reactions are resolved in order of stage (lowest stage first).
- After all reactions resolve, the next player's turn begins.

### Circuit

A **circuit** is one complete rotation — every player has taken one turn. At the end
of each circuit:
- If the Officiant still holds the highest stage, they earn +1 Challenge token.
- Check whether the Officiant role passes (see §8 Officiant).

## 5. Actions

### 1. Move
Walk to any zone in the room. You may pass through other zones without stopping.
Players in zones you pass through do not react to your movement.
**Cost:** Free. Always available as part of your turn.

### 2. Perform
Execute a physical act belonging to your current stage's act type.
You must be in a zone to perform. Your act should be visible to all players.
Acts are physical and improvised — no words, no props. Duration: 3–10 seconds.

| Stage | Act type | Core quality | Typical gestures |
|---|---|---|---|
| Novice | **Receiving** | Openness, expectation | Cupped palms up; open arms; kneeling; head back |
| Apprentice | **Witnessing** | Fixed attention, acknowledgment | Turning fully to face; hand on heart; slow nod |
| Initiate | **Offering** | Extension, giving | Raised hands forward; bowing with arms out; presenting |
| Elder | **Completing** | Stillness, finality | Clasped hands, eyes closed; 3 seconds absolute stillness; final bow |

**Advancement:** If you perform your current stage's act type in your stage's advance
zone, and you are not Interrupted (see §8), you advance. Take the next Stage card
from the Stage Pile, swap it privately for your current card, and return your old
card face-down to the pile.

**Performing a different stage's act type:** You may perform an act that belongs to
a different stage — as misdirection or error. This does NOT advance you. It may
trigger reactions from players whose stage responds to that act type.

### 3. Call
Spend 1 Challenge token. Stand and declare: *"[Player name] is a [stage]."*
- **Correct:** The called player shows their Stage card. They regress one stage
  (swap their card for the previous stage's card from the pile). You gain 1 Score
  token. If the called player was the Officiant, the Officiant token passes
  immediately (see §8).
- **Incorrect:** You gain 1 negative marker. The Challenge token is spent regardless.
- You may not Call the Officiant during their own entry phase (Phase 1).
- You may not Call yourself.

### 4. Defer
Instead of performing, you step toward any player in any zone and perform a
Witnessing act directed at them. You gain nothing directly — but Deferring when
another player is in a zone you need can occupy the zone safely without revealing
your stage. Deferring also fulfills any reaction obligation you have for this turn.

### 5. Convene *(Officiant only)*
Once per circuit, the Officiant may stand and declare: *"The ceremony calls."*
All players must move to the zone the Officiant designates within 10 seconds.
The Officiant then performs any act in that zone. All players in that zone must
perform Deference. This is the Officiant's strongest tool — it can displace a player
who is about to advance, forcing them out of their advance zone.
**Cost:** Once per circuit. Announced aloud.

## 6. Scoring / End Condition

### Win Condition
The ceremony ends immediately when a player performs a valid Completing act in the
Sanctum zone while holding an Elder Stage card and is **not successfully Called**
during or immediately after that act.

After the completing act, any player may immediately spend a Challenge token to Call
*"[Player] is not an Elder."* If correct (the player is not at Elder stage), the
win is voided. If incorrect, the calling player gains a negative marker and the
win stands. This creates a brief dramatic window — the potential winner holds still;
the room decides whether to challenge.

### Score Tokens (secondary)
Used to determine second and third place after the ceremony winner is declared.

| Event | Score |
|---|---|
| Correct Call | +1 token |
| Wrong Call | −1 marker |
| Held Officiant role for 2+ consecutive circuits | +1 token (bonus, awarded at game end) |
| Final win (completing act succeeds) | ceremony winner; token tally determines placement among non-winners |

### Tiebreaker
Most Challenge tokens remaining (unused) among tied players.

## 7. Edge Cases & Clarifications

**Q: Can two players be in the same zone?**
Yes. Zones are not exclusive. Multiple players performing simultaneously in one zone
creates a rich reaction environment.

**Q: Can you perform a Completing act anywhere, or only in the Sanctum?**
Completing acts can be performed anywhere as misdirection. They only trigger a win
when performed in the Sanctum by an Elder. Performing a Completing act elsewhere as
a non-Elder is a valid misdirection but triggers reactions from Initiates (who react
to Elder acts).

**Q: What if the Officiant tries to Convene players into the Sanctum?**
Allowed. This is a high-risk Officiant move — if an Elder is in the Sanctum and
performs a Completing act during the Convene, they may win on the Officiant's turn.
The Officiant takes this risk by choosing the Sanctum.

**Q: Can a player refuse to react?**
No. Reactions are involuntary by design and by social contract. A player who refuses
to react when their trigger fires has violated the game's implicit norm. The group
should restart the ceremony from the current state if this happens.

**Q: What if all players are Elders?**
Rare but possible at 3p. The first Elder to reach the Sanctum and complete wins.
The reaction chain becomes Elder→Elder (Elders react to Initiate acts — so no
reactions fire between Elders, and the final race is pure positional).

**Q: What if someone runs out of Challenge tokens?**
They cannot Call for the rest of the game unless they receive additional tokens.
No other source of tokens exists in the base game.

**Q: Can the Officiant Call during their own turn?**
Yes — spending a Challenge token to Call is a valid turn action (replaces Perform).
The Officiant may also Call as a reaction to another player's act (outside their
turn) at any time, spending a Challenge token.

**Q: Does Deferring satisfy your turn completely?**
Yes. Deferring is a complete turn — you do not also Move and Perform.

**Q: What happens when a player at Novice stage is Called incorrectly as a higher
stage?**
Standard outcome: caller gains a negative marker. The Novice does NOT reveal their
card — the Call was wrong, so the ceremony's information economy is preserved.

## 8. Special Subsystems

### The Reaction Chain

Reactions are involuntary. When any player Performs, check: does this act type
trigger any other player's reaction?

**Reaction trigger table:**

| Performing player's act | Reacts |
|---|---|
| Receiving (Novice act) | Apprentices |
| Witnessing (Apprentice act) | Initiates |
| Offering (Initiate act) | Elders |
| Completing (Elder act) | No one (no stage is above Elder) |

**How to react:** Stand up. Move to the performing player's zone (or take one step
toward them if already adjacent). Perform a brief Witnessing act directed at them
(3 seconds). Then return to your position.

**What a reaction reveals:** Your reaction confirms you are the stage that reacts to
that act type. If you react to a Receiving act, you are (probably) an Apprentice.
This is the game's information leak. Skilled players minimize how explicitly their
reactions confirm their stage — by reacting ambiguously (is that a Witnessing act or
just paying attention?). The social contract: the reaction must be genuine enough
to be visible. Half-reactions are bad form.

**Multiple simultaneous reactions:** If multiple players must react to the same act,
they react in stage order (lowest stage first). Higher-stage reactions can then
respond to what they see in lower-stage reactions — the chain can compound.

### The Officiant

**Holding the role:** The player at the highest stage holds the Officiant token
visibly. All players know who the Officiant is; they do not know the Officiant's
exact stage — only that they are currently the most senior.

**Tenure:** The Officiant holds the role for one full circuit after claiming it.
After that circuit, if another player has advanced to a higher stage, the token
passes immediately. If the Officiant is successfully Called and regresses, the
token passes immediately to the next-highest player.

**Officiant entry — Deference:** When the Officiant enters a zone where another
player is currently performing or about to perform, all players already in that
zone must stop and perform a Deference act before the Officiant performs:
- Stand facing the Officiant
- Perform a Witnessing act directed at them (3–5 seconds)
- Then resume normal play

Deference is mandatory. It temporarily freezes all advancement attempts in that zone.
The Officiant cannot trigger Deference in the Sanctum when a player is mid-Completing
act (the win moment is protected from interruption by rule — see §7).

**Officiant rewards:** +1 Challenge token per circuit held. The Officiant becomes
more powerful the longer they hold the role — but also more conspicuous.

**Officiant identification:** Holding the Officiant token confirms you are currently
the most senior player — but NOT your specific stage. A player who reaches Initiate
and takes the token from a former Initiate has revealed only that they are now the
most senior, not whether they are Initiate or (implausibly) Elder.

### Stage Advancement — Full Protocol

1. Player Performs their stage's act type in their stage's advance zone.
2. No Interruption fires (no Convene from Officiant; zone not blocked by active
   Deference from a prior action this turn).
3. Player discreetly takes the next Stage card from the Stage Pile and replaces
   their current card (face-down swap — should appear natural; players may practice
   this).
4. If the advancing player is now at the highest stage overall, they take the
   Officiant token immediately (or retain it if they already held it).
5. No announcement. No confirmation. The room infers from subsequent reactions.

**Failed advancement:** If you perform the right act in the right zone but are
Interrupted (by Officiant Convene or zone being cleared by Deference), your
advancement attempt is void. You must perform the act again on a future turn.

## 9. Variants

### 3-Player Setup
Use 4 zones instead of 5 (omit the Passage zone — draw from the remaining 11 cards).
The Altar and Liminal zones are adjacent (closer together than in 5p). Each player
starts with 4 Challenge tokens (one extra — the information environment is thinner).

### 6-Player Setup
Use all 5 zones. Each player starts with 2 Challenge tokens (scarcity is the 6p
balance lever — the room is information-rich; Calling is more dangerous). Add a
second Passage zone (draw from remaining variant cards) to create more neutral space.

### Open Stage Variant (introductory)
Each player places their current Stage card face-up in front of them when not actively
performing. You know everyone's stage at a glance. Reactions still fire. The game
becomes pure positional race — who can advance first while the Officiant disrupts.
Recommended for first-time players to learn the physical vocabulary.

### Blind Officiant Variant (advanced)
The Officiant token is not passed openly. The Officiant holds it hidden (in pocket).
The room must deduce who is Officiant from who exercises Officiant powers (Convene,
Entry). The Officiant may bluff — not entering zones, not Convening — to conceal
their seniority. The Officiant cannot be Called as Officiant; they can only be Called
on their specific stage. For players who find the base game's information too legible.

## 10. Designer Notes

**Why involuntary reactions?**
Every game with hidden information allows players to poker-face their way through.
RITE removes that option for the reaction layer. You cannot choose not to react when
your trigger fires — the game's social contract requires it, and the room enforces it.
This involuntariness is where A26 lives: you agreed, by sitting down, to be honest
about your reactions. The norm cannot be written into the rules; it has to be
accepted.

**Why the Officiant disrupts rather than scores?**
The Officiant's power is negative — they freeze, they interrupt, they force Deference.
This is intentional. An Officiant who could score directly would become a runaway
leader. Instead, the Officiant's power is to slow everyone else. Combined with their
exposure (everyone watches the most senior player), the role is powerful and
precarious simultaneously.

**Why Challenge tokens instead of open Calling?**
Free Calling creates a degenerate strategy: spam Calls on the leader every turn and
regress them continuously. Challenge tokens make Calling a resource decision. You
have 3 uses; spend them on high-confidence reads. The Officiant earning extra tokens
is a small compensation for their visibility — they need more Calls because everyone
is watching them specifically.

**The Deference information leak:**
When you perform Deference to the Officiant, you are performing a Witnessing act.
Witnessing is the Apprentice's advance act. Initiates react to Witnessing acts.
So: if an Initiate is in the zone when you Defer to the Officiant, they must react
to your Deference. Your mandatory respectful act triggers their mandatory reaction.
The hierarchy creates cascading information revelations by design. The ceremony
unfolds whether you want it to or not.

## 12. Open Questions at DESIGN Stage

1. **Can the Officiant strip rank directly?** Current design: no — only Calls can
   regress players. But the Officiant's Convene can void an advancement attempt,
   which is indirect rank-denial. An optional variant: the Officiant may spend 2
   Challenge tokens to make a "Formal Challenge" — a Call at the same cost as 2
   regular Calls, but with a bonus: if correct, the regressed player also loses all
   their Challenge tokens (double punishment). This is the rank-stripping card.
   Test without it first.

2. **Trigger specificity for reactions:** Current design uses act TYPE (Receiving,
   Witnessing, Offering, Completing) rather than specific gestures. This means a
   player performing a very ambiguous act might not trigger reactions because others
   cannot determine its type. Is this a feature (skilled misdirection) or a bug
   (unresolvable disputes)? Test first — if disputes arise, add zone specificity
   (reactions fire only when act is in specific zone types).

3. **Advancement speed calibration:** At 3p, the race to Elder could be too fast
   (fewer players = fewer reaction sources = faster advancement). Does the advance
   zone requirement need to change at 3p (e.g., must advance in a zone where another
   player is present as a witness)? Test first.

4. **Elder final act duration:** "3 seconds of absolute stillness" is the current
   standard. Is 3 seconds long enough to be dramatic but short enough not to be
   ridiculous? Alternative: the Elder must hold until the Officiant performs a
   Witnessing act toward them (the Officiant acknowledges the completion). This
   makes the win condition collaborative between winner and Officiant — the Officiant
   cannot prevent the win but must formally witness it.

5. **Deference in the Sanctum:** If the Officiant enters the Sanctum while an Elder
   is mid-Completing act, the win is protected (§7 ruling). But what if the
   Officiant enters before the Elder begins? Current ruling: Deference fires, Elder
   must wait. This is a legitimate Officiant power — they can be in the Sanctum to
   watch for the win attempt and potentially call it wrong.

6. **Score token endgame:** After the ceremony winner is declared, secondary scoring
   takes at most 30 seconds (count tokens). Does secondary scoring add meaningful
   play, or should RITE be winner-takes-all? Test both; winner-takes-all is simpler
   and may be correct for a 35-minute game.

7. **Officiant token visibility:** The Officiant must visibly hold/wear the token.
   What if a player pockets it? Social contract covers this — but a physical design
   that makes concealment impossible (a worn bracelet, a hat) may be worth the
   component cost.
