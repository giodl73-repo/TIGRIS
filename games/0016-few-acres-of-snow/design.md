# A Few Acres of Snow — Design Import (fair-use rules summary)

French & Indian War colonial conflict (1688–1760s abstraction). 2 players (British vs French). ~90 min. BGG weight 3.2.

## Core loop

Each turn: **play 2 cards from hand** (5-card hand), **refill**, opponent turn. No action rounds — each card performs one of:
- **Settle** (build fort/village/city on adjacent location, gain location card into deck)
- **Besiege** (attack enemy location; requires military cards)
- **Ambush** (play matching military card from hand; extra cost but nullifies sieges)
- **Develop** (buy a card from the central market — adds cards to personal deck)
- **Trigger special** (card-text abilities: Indian raid, trader, administrator, etc.)
- **Reserve** (set card aside for later retrieval — deck-management)
- **Discard two cards** for a bonus action (cycling)

## Deck-building dimension

Starting deck ~10 cards per side (location cards for starting territories + basic actions).

- **Settling adds location cards** to your deck (permanent).
- **Development buys** from 8 purchasable card-types (trader, military, governor, etc.).
- **Siege cards** consumed per besiege action.
- **Reserve mechanic** = take a card out of cycle for later use.
- **No discard pile shuffle** — reshuffle happens when deck empties.

## Map

~30 locations across North American east coast (Quebec, Montreal, Boston, New York, Halifax, etc.). Each location has victory value (1-3 VP) and location-card in deck.

## Victory

- **Instant win**: control opponent's capital OR hold 18+ VP at end of turn.
- **Siege of capital** = instant war win.
- **Late-game VP lead** = economic/settlement win.

## Asymmetry

- **British**: denser east coast; strong settler cards; Halifax key city.
- **French**: broader west/north reach; military advantages; raids and Indian-ally cards.

## The Halifax Hammer

**Known solution:** British first-player settles Halifax turn 1, then uses Boston/NY location cards + governor + trader combo to flood deck with low-cost-high-value cards faster than French can respond. French win rate at expert level effectively 0.

Wallace acknowledged the exploit in interviews; no official fix published. This is AFoS's famous lesson — a deck-building + area-control game can be *solved* when opening-deck shape + card-acquisition rate has a dominated path.

## Design signatures

- **Card triple-use** — each location card is: location reference, settling tool, and deck-building resource.
- **Ambush vs Siege asymmetric engagement** — attacker pays, defender may void via hand card.
- **Reserve mechanic** — deck cycling without shuffle penalty.
- **Location-as-card** — geographic map nodes enter your deck when controlled.
- **No shuffle-triggered events** — reshuffle is pure.

## Design failure (for review context)

**Halifax Hammer is structural evidence for A7 Emergence refute.** A game that solves at expert level has **degenerate emergence**: strategy space collapses to one dominant path.
