# Rosenberg on UNFOLD — concept review

## Opening verdict

I traced this system three times before writing. UNFOLD is not a Rosenberg-shaped design — it is a spatial-expression game with an aesthetic judgment at the end, closer in spine to Carcassonne than to Le Havre. That's fine. I review it against its own ambition, per the rubric. But my lens is scarcity and engine-gardens, and what I need to know is whether each pipeline stage bites, whether the asymmetric tools compose, and whether the 2-action flip carries enough weight to make placement regret load-bearing. The concept is patient and clearly written. It earns a hearing. It does not yet earn my signature axes, and the gap is specific: there is no feeding-equivalent, no periodic obligation that converts abundance into shortage, and the asymmetric tools are modifiers rather than engine stages that gear into each other. The design is betting its depth on *interpretation* of a shared canvas rather than on *conversion* across a chain. That bet can pay, but the concept does not yet show me how.

## Three concerns

**1. Scarcity ledger is underwritten.** Three candidate scarcities exist: the tile bag (~60 tiles), Spirit tokens (one per player), and the 3-action turn budget. None of them is a feeding-equivalent. The bag depletes, but depletion ends the game — it does not periodically punish neglect mid-arc. Spirits are binary, not scarce across a gradient. Three actions per turn is a budget, not a shortage; without a contested action board (Agricola's scarce slots), players don't compete for the *same* action, they only compete for the same *tile on the board*. Question 2 of my diagnostic: at 2p with 60 tiles and ~40 placements, does anyone feel starved of anything? I suspect not. C3 will be hard to earn without a refresh-and-obligation beat.

**2. Engine-garden is absent by construction.** Each culture has one Tool (modifier on PLACE or FLIP) and one Spirit. That's two asymmetric pieces. They don't gear into each other across turns the way an occupation-plus-improvement deck does in Agricola, or the way Le Havre's buildings compound through mid-game. A Permaculture player's free-tree-place doesn't unlock a second capability at turn 8; it just keeps being that one capability. The design is asymmetric at start and asymmetric at end, but the middle is flat. Question 4 of my diagnostic asks for three upgrades whose value is conditional on other placements — UNFOLD's answer is "tiles," and tiles are shared, so the conditionality is social rather than architectural.

**3. Flip weight is under-specified.** A 2-action flip against a 3-action turn is 66% of your round. That is heavy. But because flipping can be done on *any* tile, the strategic surface of the flip depends entirely on whether opposing culture-lenses have meaningfully divergent valuations on the *same* tile in different states. If the cultures genuinely disagree across enough state-pairs, flipping is a real weapon. If they mostly ignore each other (Indigenous doesn't care about a Modernist road; Modernist doesn't care about a preserved forest), flipping collapses into self-interested placement and the interaction axis drains out.

## Two greenlights

**1. Variable starting seed + six cultures composes replayability honestly.** 3-4 scenario configurations × 6 cultures choose 2-4 is a real combinatorial space. This is the Agricola-occupation-deck move done structurally rather than through cards. It is the part of the concept that most closely mirrors my own practice.

**2. Shared-island constraint is principled.** No individual player boards, one canvas, all flips public — this forces interaction through physical state rather than through auction or trade. It is the opposite of my usual worker-placement grammar, but it is internally coherent and it earns D2 Spatial-Interaction Presence honestly.

## One revision

**Add a mid-game obligation.** Introduce a Dusk phase every N placements: each player must *spend* a Spirit-adjacency worth of score, or lose their Tool until they reclaim a Temple. This converts the Spirit from a scoring multiplier into a tempo resource and gives the design a clock that bites. Without this or an equivalent, C3 Scarcity Bite stays a 4.

## Scores

- **B5 Architectural Novelty:** 8/10 at 3-4p. Pop-up physics + culture-lens + shared island has no sibling I can name. The concept's anchor bet is defensible.
- **C3 Scarcity Bite:** 4/10 at 3-4p. No feeding-equivalent; tile bag depletes without mid-arc obligation; 3-action budget is loose. Needs a Dusk-phase or equivalent to reach 7.
- **C4 Engine-Garden Dependency:** 3/10 at 3-4p. Two asymmetric pieces per culture do not gear across turns. Shared tiles substitute social conditionality for architectural conditionality; the substitution does not pay my axis.
