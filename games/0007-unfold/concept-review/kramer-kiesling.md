# Kramer-Kiesling on UNFOLD

*"A nine-year-old should be able to flip a tile and see the board change. Whether she understands why six cultures value that flip differently is a separate question — and the one that decides this design."*

## The action-point bone is right

Three actions per turn, costs 1 (place) or 2 (flip or move Spirit), shared board — that is the Tikal/Torres/Mexica skeleton, and we recognize our own handwriting in it. The menu is small: **place, flip, move Spirit, protect**. Iconography load per turn is low. The opportunity cost between "place a fresh tile" and "flip one the Modernist just opened" is the correct strategic pressure. This half of UNFOLD teaches in five minutes.

The pop-up component is not an obstacle to teach — it is an aid. A 3-state tile whose state is its physical orientation **replaces a rules page with a hinge**. Azul's factory display teaches colour-take by being looked at; UNFOLD's hinged tile teaches state-flip the same way. Components carrying rules is the teachable move.

## Where the design load spikes

Six cultures, each with (a) a Tool modifier, (b) a Spirit rule, and (c) a bespoke endgame scoring lens, is **three rule-layers stacked on the same card**. That is the stress-point. A player reading her own card learns three things; a player reading the board must partially model five others. At 4p the opponent-lens inference cost is real. A Permaculture player cannot plan her tree-adjacency placement without asking "does this also score for Medieval, who is to my left and ahead?"

This is not a teach problem. The 15-minute teach is plausible **if** each player reads only her own culture card at teach time and the other cards stay face-down until scoring. That is the family-layer read. The expert-layer read — tracking what each opponent's lens values — is where depth hides. Good family-to-expert split, provided the rulebook explicitly tells the teacher: "do not explain the other five cultures."

## Spatial interaction is strong

Flipping opponents' tiles for 2 actions is direct board-state coupling of the Mexica canal-blocking sort. The Spirit-on-Temple claim is tile-adjacency scoring made visible on the component. Three-dimensional verticality (open-up state) means spatial relations read from across the table — no one has to lean over to see who claimed what. This is the spatial-interaction axis at its intended weight.

One concern: asymmetric scoring can leak into **multiplayer-parallel-scoring** where each player optimises her own lens and ignores the shared board. The flip cost (2 actions) must be cheap enough that flipping-to-spite is a real move at every player count. If flipping is ever economically irrational, the shared board becomes six private puzzles.

## Stakes

### C7 Action-Menu Clarity — **8.0**
Menu size four (place, flip, move Spirit, protect), costs 1–2, state encoded in component orientation. The per-turn icon load is Azul-tier. Deducting two points for the culture-card asymmetry, which adds a private sub-menu per player.

### D1 Family-to-Expert Scaling — **7.5**
Family layer: place tiles, flip tiles, score your own lens — a 9-year-old can play. Expert layer: predict six lenses' interactions, Spirit displacement timing, flip economy. The split is honest. Score loses a point because six cultures is one or two more than the family layer can carry at 4p; we would cut to four in base and sell the other two as an expansion, as with Azul Stained Glass vs. Azul.

### D2 Spatial-Interaction Presence — **8.5**
Shared island, physical 3D state, flip-opponents action, Spirit-claim-by-adjacency. Direct coupling at every placement. The open-up vertical state makes the board legible from all seats, which is an axis-lifting component choice. Asara's tower-building reached 7; this reaches further because the flip action directly mutates opponents' scoring geometry.

### A6 Teachability — **7.0**
The core turn teaches in under 10 minutes. The culture cards at 4p add 5 minutes if taught in parallel, which lands the full teach at the 15-minute claim exactly — no margin. Rulebook must commit to "teach your own card only." If first-play confusion is recorded above turn 3, score drops to 6.

## Verdict

UNFOLD's action-point spine and spatial-interaction design are in our tradition and defended well. The pop-up tile is the teachable component we would have wanted for Tikal if cardboard engineering had allowed. The risk is culture-count: six lenses is the ceiling, and at 4p the lens-inference load may crowd out the placement decision that is the game's centre. Cut to four in base, extend via expansion.
