# Feld on ZEN PATH

Every turn should give you something — and something you didn't ask for. That is the Feld test. ZEN PATH hands me a clean single-action turn and asks me to bless it. I'll bless half of it.

## A2 Decision Density — the north star

**3p: 6.5. 4p: 7.0.**

One action per turn is the right shape for a 35-45 minute race. I am not against terseness. Samurai gives you one action per turn and the decision load sits at a 9. So the question is not *how many actions*, it is *how many conversions each action enables*.

Here is where the draft-then-play structure earns its keep. A single drafted tile can be read three ways before it touches the table: move me toward Peak, raise my MIN-aspect, or drop a gate that locks an opponent's approach. That is three live conversions per draft, gated by a public 5-slot market. I'll take it. The market pressure (5 slots, refill from bag) means the tile I want is a tile someone else also wants — so the *something I didn't ask for* arrives as the market shape after my draft closes.

Red flag at 3p: with 40 squares and no cascade, mid-game turns (positions 15-25, aspects 3-4) risk flattening. If the market offers two Step-1s and a redundant Ascend, the player has taken an action but made no decision. Count those turns in playtest. If >10%, the density claim fails.

The draft-then-play structure does meet my test — tentatively. It needs the market churn rate to stay above the player's plan cycle. If players can plan two turns ahead reliably, density collapses to execution.

## C6 Point-Salad Incommensurability — or the opposite

**3p: 3.5. 4p: 3.5.**

Here I have to call out the real problem. C6 and C2 (Knizia's anchor axis) are arguably *opposite* ends of the scoring design space. Point-salad says: score from every direction, and let the player harvest which bonuses this turn. Minimum-score shape says: score from one direction, and punish the player who didn't hedge across all of them. Castles of Burgundy versus Samurai.

ZEN PATH has chosen Samurai. The scoring equation is one line: `position × MIN(H, S, S)`. That is C2 fully committed — and it earns C2 canonically. But it forfeits C6 by construction. The three aspects are not incommensurable in the Burgundy sense; they are three instances of the same good (aspect-points) filtered through a min-gate. You are not choosing between ship-VP and livestock-VP; you are filling a bathtub with three equal-diameter taps and the level is set by the lowest.

Compare:
- **Castles of Burgundy** — ship, livestock, building, worker. Each pays on its own curve. You choose which curve to ride.
- **Trajan** — six sub-games, each with its own reward gradient. Six incommensurable systems.
- **Macao** — wind rose generates cubes whose timing reshapes scoring. Two incommensurable axes (timing × color).
- **ZEN PATH** — one curve (position × MIN-aspect). One axis.

This does not make ZEN PATH a weak design. It makes it an *explicitly non-Feld* design. The question is whether Samurai's tight shape survives the cross with Chutes & Ladders. Samurai is a five-aspect city-influence auction with 3 colors that matter plus 1 that doesn't plus ronin. Its richness comes from the *spatial tile-placement economy around the cities*, not from the min-scoring alone. ZEN PATH keeps the min and drops the spatial auction economy. That is thin.

So: ZEN PATH properly inherits Samurai's *shape*, but it does not inherit Samurai's *richness*. The richness in Samurai sits in the influence-tile placement geometry; ZEN PATH replaces that geometry with a linear path and three aspect tracks. Min-score without the auction.

## Implicit C5 reference

C5 is retired. I note for the record: the chutes here should not pretend to be catastrophe phases. A chute that fires on Strength-lowest is not In-the-Year-of-the-Dragon's plague phase; it is a local penalty. Do not claim C5 weight the game has not earned. Chutes land on individuals, not the table.

## Verdict

Honor the Knizia anchor; do not reach for Feld-shape on top of it. If the user wants C6 alongside C2, the scoring equation needs a second incommensurable track — which Samurai does not have either. Ship it as Samurai, not Burgundy.
