# Lacerda on ZEN PATH

I come to a track-race with aspect gates expecting to find a machine. What I find is a schematic. Let me trace the gearing before I score.

## The Lisboa test

Four subsystems: **path position**, **aspect tracks**, **ladder/chute placement**, **tile market**. I run the removal test.

Pull the player-placed ladders/chutes (12 of 55 tiles). What happens? Path position still advances from Step tiles. Aspects still rise from Ascend/Focus. The market still churns. The game survives — it just becomes a tile-draft race with fixed board features. That is the Lisboa test failing. In Lisboa, remove the ship clock and commerce collapses, politics collapses, the officials' market collapses. Here, the placement subsystem is a garnish, not a gear.

Run it again on aspect tracks. Remove aspects entirely and the min-score equation collapses — so aspects are load-bearing. Good. But aspects gear into *only one* thing: the scoring multiplier and the ladder/chute triggers. The path geometry itself does not care what your Honor is. Compare Kanban EV, where the design department gears into production, which gears into shipment, which gears back into admin. Three-way mutual dependency. Here I see path → aspects → scoring as a **linear chain**, not a mesh.

Run it on the tile market. Remove the market and hand it tiles from a bag. The game barely notices. The market is a pacing device, not a system.

The subsystems **add**. They do not multiply. That is the concept's structural weakness on B1.

## Information transparency — feature or weakness?

The non-goals list says: "No hidden information. All tiles, aspects, positions public." The concept frames this as commitment. I frame it as **surrender of an axis**.

I design with transparency because my asymmetric engines *already* generate enough interaction density that hidden information would occlude the puzzle. The player is fighting the system, not the opponent's hand. ZEN PATH is a 40-square race with 3 aspects and an open market. There is not enough interior to occlude. Full transparency here means the optimal move is **computable** at each turn. Three plays in, a competent group will solve the tile-weight-to-aspect ratio. B4 Information-Transparency-Cost at anchor 10 requires that information is an *active economy* — players paying to learn things. ZEN PATH has no such economy. Information is free; therefore B4 is not an axis this game engages with, it is an axis this game abandons.

That's a strategic error for a design that wants Knizia depth. Samurai (1998) has hidden tiles behind a screen. Taj Mahal has hidden card hands. The min-score-shape canon *uses* hidden information to protect the puzzle from solution. ZEN PATH strips that protection and hopes the geometry compensates. It will not.

## The 10th play

Concept predicts "A7 refute: tile pool fixed per game; replayability moderate." I agree and go further. The strategic space of this design is:

1. Which aspect to push first (3 options).
2. When to switch from aspect-building to path-advance.
3. Where to place your 1-2 player-ladders/chutes.

That is a **3-axis optimization over a 40-turn arc** with full information. Veterans will converge on a dominant curve within 5-10 plays: push two aspects to 5 each by turn 10, advance aggressively while the third aspect creeps up via Focus tiles. The min-score geometry *forces* this — the scoring equation has a visible optimum. On Mars rewards its 10th play because the terraforming track, the corporation contracts, the shuttle, and the dependencies *interact non-linearly*. ZEN PATH's equation is `position × min(H,S,St)`. That equation has a first-order solution.

## Scores (3p-4p design target)

**B1 System Gearing: 3.** Subsystems add. Removing player-placed ladders/chutes leaves a viable game — the definitive refutation of multiplicative dependency. The Lisboa test fails.

**B4 Information-Transparency Cost: 2.** The design commits to zero hidden information as a feature. On an axis that measures what hidden info *costs*, a zero-hidden-info game scores near the floor. Not a Lacerda-bad stance universally — it is bad *for this design's depth ambition*.

**A7 Emergence/Replayability: 4.** 5-10 play ceiling on strategic expansion. Fixed tile pool, transparent state, solvable optimum. The min-score shape protects against *pure* rush, but it does not generate the expanding strategy arc a 10-play game demands.

## What would fix it

Gear the aspects into the path geometry itself (aspect-coloured squares whose value depends on the samurai holding a matching aspect lead). Add a hidden-tile draft layer — Samurai had a screen for a reason. Make the 12 placement tiles load-bearing — give them residual scoring that fires at endgame on non-adjacent conditions, so removing them breaks scoring. Without these, this is a 45-minute puzzle with a clever scoring equation wearing a samurai costume.

Knizia will score it higher. He is right on the equation. I am right on the machine.
