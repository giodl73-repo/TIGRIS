# Stegmaier review — ZEN PATH (concept)

*If a new player can't make an interesting decision on their first turn, we haven't finished the design.*

I'm reading ZEN PATH with an accessibility-first lens. The Knizia anchor is clean and the one-action turn is a real asset — but the concept is quietly betting that three systems (aspect tracks, tile market, path geometry) will feel like one system. That's the bet I'm stress-testing, against Scythe, Wingspan, and Viticulture as my yardsticks for iconography-first production and player-count robustness.

## A6 Teachability — 6/10 at 3-4p, score pending at 2p

The rules teach is short on paper: draft a tile, play a tile, check if you hit a path feature, refill. That's comparable to a Wingspan first-round teach and shorter than Scythe's teach. Good.

What worries me is not the **verbal** teach — it's the **iconographic** teach, which is where a Stegmaier-lens is strictest. Each tile has to encode: (a) type, (b) effect, (c) for ladder/chute tiles the *three aspect-threshold options* a player picks from at placement. That last one is the teachability risk. A Wingspan bird card is dense but it commits to one effect; a ZEN PATH Ladder tile is a **decision shell**, and the player must understand the shell before they can draft it. That pushes the iconography from "reference sheet" (Stegmaier greenlight) to "decision tree on a tile" (Stegmaier red flag).

Recommendation for the design stage: the Ladder and Chute tiles need a single unified icon grammar for "Honor ≥ X / Strength ≥ X / Strategy ≥ X" that matches the aspect-track icons (scroll / sword / fan). If those five glyphs — three aspect icons + ≥ + numeral — are shared across tiles, path features, and aspect tracks, the iconography becomes the reference sheet. That's the Scythe-player-mat move and it's the only way I'll score A6 above 7 at 3-4p.

## C8 First-Turn Compression — 7/10, contingent on player-aid

The first turn of ZEN PATH is genuinely compressed in the good sense. A new player sees: five tiles in the market, their samurai at Origin, aspects at 0 (or 2 — open question #7). The scoring equation `position × MIN(aspects)` is the game's whole strategic thesis and it is visible on turn 1. That's a Scythe-quality compression move: the first action teaches what the game is about.

The caveat is the min-score reveal. Knizia's Samurai has this same problem and solves it with a card-width player-aid that just restates "your worst region wins." ZEN PATH's min-score rule is the scoring spine, but a first-time player will default to a C&L-race reading — advance fast, grab ladders, win. The design has to actively re-educate on turn 1. The player-aid card must show the scoring equation as the **first** line, not buried under tile reference. If the player-aid leads with tiles, compression fails and the game teaches "race" before it teaches "balance."

Player-aid card sizing: 3 aspect icons + 7 tile types + the scoring equation on a tarot-sized card is tight but achievable (Viticulture's summer/winter card is this density). It fits if the tile types share effect-icons with the path features, which they should anyway.

## D3 Count-Robustness — 4/10 as concept

This is my blocker-level concern and it's the axis TIGRIS flagged me as primary advocate on. The non-goals section states: "No solo mode in v1.0." Two-player is listed as "variant." Design target is 3-4.

Against Scythe (1-7 all tuned, Automa ships with the base game) and Wingspan (1-5 with an Automa), ZEN PATH as scoped is a narrow-count design. At 2p the tile market (5 slots, refill-from-bag) starves of opposition-pressure: with only one opponent your ladder/chute placements don't compound. At solo, the Knizia min-score shape is fine as a puzzle but there's no Duel tile interaction and no player-placed chute threat, which removes two of the seven tile types from functional play.

Blocker severity:
- **Solo missing:** I score this a soft blocker. The min-score shape ports to solo with a target-score puzzle (beat 120, beat 150) more cleanly than Scythe ported to Automa. Not shipping solo is a choice, not an impossibility — but every Stegmaier-lens reviewer will note it as a red flag per my rubric.
- **2p as variant:** Hard blocker. "Variant" means untuned. A 3-4p target with 2p-as-variant is the exact pattern I flag in Red Flags #3 ("player counts that feel like afterthoughts"). Fix: promote 2p to first-class with a 30-square path and 45-tile pool, test it as part of DESIGN, not as an expansion.

## Production note

The winding-path board as centerpiece is a Viticulture-style load-bearing production bet. Sumi-e zigzag is evocative and the chutes-as-precipice, ladders-as-temple-stairs iconographic layer reinforces mechanics through theme. Tactile greenlight — provided the aspect icons on tracks, tiles, and path features are literally the same glyphs.

## Summary

Knizia anchor is clean. One-action turn keeps pacing tight. First-turn compression is achievable with a disciplined player-aid. Teachability is a toss-up that depends entirely on iconographic consolidation of the aspect glyphs. Count-robustness is the axis where this concept is currently weakest and where I'd push hardest during DESIGN: promote 2p, scope a solo target-score mode, retest the market's refill dynamics at each count.
