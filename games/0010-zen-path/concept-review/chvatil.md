# Chvátil on ZEN PATH — concept review

## Opening verdict

The designer is honest, and I respect honesty: this is a combinatorial design — race geometry crossed with a minimum-aspect scoring shape — and the concept-doc says so on line 118. Good. The question I'm here to ask is whether the one bet the designer DOES make — *aspect-gated jumps replacing dice* — is load-bearing, or merely a clever substitution that any Euro designer in 1998 could have written on a napkin. My honest read: it is a substitution, not an architecture. Thebes already gates track-race events by resource levels. Abyss already threads influence-tracks through a card-draft. What ZEN PATH proposes is the delta between those and Samurai's min-aspect scoring — a real delta, but not a novel structural bet in the Tzolk'in-gears sense. The architecture is comfortable. Too comfortable.

## Three concerns

**C1. The aspect-gate replacement is a skin on Thebes' resource-gated events (B5).** Thebes gates dig-outcomes on your knowledge-track state; the jumps trigger or not based on what you've invested. ZEN PATH gates jumps on aspect thresholds. Same shape: "a track event fires iff your state variable crosses a line." The axis question I keep pointing at: *remove the aspect-gating and substitute a d6-with-mitigation, does any other game become indistinguishable from this one?* I think the answer is "Abyss with a winding board." That's damning.

**C2. No load-bearing weirdness (B5).** Compare the designer's own reference list — Tzolk'in's gears MOVE the workers. Galaxy Trucker's build phase is real-time and the rest is resolution. Through the Ages runs on a ledger that is itself the scoring lens. Every one of my games has a mechanic whose removal destroys the game's interior. Remove ZEN PATH's tile-draft-to-aspect-gate loop — you have Samurai with movement. Remove the min-score scoring — you have a track race with state-gated jumps. Both remainders are playable. Neither piece is structurally dependent on the other. That's a cross, not an architecture.

**C3. The interaction surface is underbuilt relative to the claim (A3).** Duel tiles (5 out of 55, ~9% of draws) and shared ladder/chute placement are real interaction, but the interaction is diffuse and optional. A 3-4 player track race where my samurai advances on my own aspect tracks and draws from a 5-slot market is mostly solitaire with occasional hostility. The designer predicts A3 earns. I doubt it at 3p and I'd pressure-test at 4p. At 2p (variant) it collapses — track-race min-score without a shared multiplier grid is Samurai-minus.

## Two greenlights

**G1. The scoring equation is genuinely good (C2, A1).** `position × MIN(aspects)` is one line, teaches in thirty seconds, and creates the decision the designer wants: *do I rush, or do I shore up?* That is a Knizia-quality spine and the designer is right to make it the centerpiece. My quibble is with the architecture around it, not the equation.

**G2. The dice-removal commitment produces real decisions (A2).** If the designer holds the line on no dice and no hidden information, every turn becomes a comprehensible optimization with live opportunity cost. That's the Codenames discipline — strip away noise until every input is legible. I approve of the austerity.

## One revision

**Make the board a Tzolk'in.** Right now the path is a static 40-square zigzag with 12 pre-printed features. Dead scenery. What if the path itself ROTATED between rounds — the printed ladders and chutes shift position each time the tile bag cycles, so a ladder you were counting on at square 27 drifts to square 24 while you were investing in Strategy? The samurai's *zen* is then literal: the path is never where you left it. That's a structural bet, not a skin. It also sharpens catastrophe pressure (B2 — which the designer concedes will refute) because a rotating hazard creates real reversal. Without something of this weight — a mechanic whose removal wrecks the game — B5 stays at 4.

## Scores

- **B5 Architectural Novelty — 3/10 at 3p; 3/10 at 4p.** The aspect-gate-for-dice swap is a substitution, not an architecture. No load-bearing weirdness; the architecture is comfortable. Designer's own concession (line 118) reinforces the score rather than softening it — refute-direction.
- **A3 Interaction — 5/10 at 3p; 6/10 at 4p.** Duel tiles + shared path + player-placed ladder/chute give real but diffuse interaction; core loop is parallel tile-drafting. Passes but does not earn.
