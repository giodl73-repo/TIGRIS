# MANIFEST — Concept

## Premise

You are a river merchant in the age of sail. Your ship is fully capable — holds deep,
crew experienced, routes well-known. But your manifest already has cargo committed: grain
contracted to the downriver mill, timber promised to the harbor works, wool bound for the
cloth market before the autumn fair. The ship isn't yours free and clear. It arrives with
obligations.

Every other merchant on this river is in the same position. You drafted your manifest at
the season's opening, choosing your commitments as carefully as you chose your ship —
knowing that the constraints you picked are the ones you can navigate, and the ones you
didn't pick might land on your rivals.

The river has one port. The port has rules: which cargo berths first, which goods pay
customs, when the deep-water channel opens, who gets the repair yard. Those rules aren't
fixed. They're set by whoever holds the harbormaster's seal — and the seal belongs to
whoever earned the authority to claim it.

MANIFEST is a game about two kinds of power that are always in conflict. Sailing earns
you income and fulfills your commitments. Being in port earns you authority and shapes
the rules your rivals must navigate. You cannot do both in the same round. Every session
begins with the same decision: go to the river, or stay at the dock.

Over a season of five rounds, you collect raw goods upriver, process them at port into
finished goods, and deliver them to downstream markets. The conversion chain — timber
to planks to furniture; grain to flour to bread; wool to cloth to garments — is long
enough that good play requires committing to a production arc early. The harbormaster
makes that arc harder or easier for everyone at the table, including themselves.

At season's end, the merchant who fulfilled the most obligations, navigated the most
constraints, and earned the most by doing both wins. Not the one who avoided the hard
manifest cards.

## Players and length

- **Players:** 2–5
- **Length:** 60–90 minutes
- **Weight:** 3.5

## Anchor mechanic

**Constraint-drafting on symmetric ships.** At setup, players snake-draft three manifest
cards each. Manifest cards are cargo commitments: *deliver X goods to Y destination by
round Z*. All ships have identical capacity and capability — strategic asymmetry comes
entirely from which obligations each player holds, not from the ship itself.

During play, the harbormaster can assign additional obligations to rivals (direct orders),
and voluntary contracts are available for players who want more income at the cost of
more commitment. The constraint landscape grows and shifts across the game; no manifest
looks the same as the one you drafted by round three.

Harbormaster authority is contested each round by a simultaneous Dock/Sail declaration.
Players who DOCK compete for the seal via influence bid; the winner plays one rule card
(standing law for the round) and may spend influence to issue one direct order. Players
who SAIL collect and deliver cargo. Influence is earned exclusively by fulfilling manifest
obligations — authority is not purchasable, it is earned.

## Table moments — the special sauce

Three designed moments that generate the stories players tell after the game:

**1. The rule that bites you back.**
Rule cards are universal — they apply to every ship on the river, including the
harbormaster's. All manifests are face-up; the table can see when you've set a rule
that constrains your own cargo. If you navigate the round successfully under a rule you
set that also cost you, you earn a bonus influence token — the game rewards making
law you can keep. The harbormaster is not above the law. This is foregrounded on every
rule card: *"This rule applies to all ships including yours."*

**2. Named obligations.**
Manifest cards carry a named party and a line of context:
*"The Widow Aldren's grain — owed before the first frost."*
*"The guild's timber — paid in advance three months past."*
*"The captain's wool — a debt of honor to House Vern."*
Constraints have faces. When the harbormaster issues a direct order, they are
commandeering a named obligation — the table hears it. When a player fails to deliver,
the unfulfilled card sits face-up on their ship mat. The Widow Aldren's grain did not
arrive. That is a story, not just a penalty.

**3. Physical commandeering.**
When the harbormaster issues a direct order, they take a cargo token from their own
supply and place it physically on the rival's ship mat. The rival's ship is now visibly
carrying the harbormaster's goods. Scoring is split: the **carrier** earns influence
for delivering (they fulfilled a commitment); the **harbormaster** scores the goods VP.
The harbormaster is gambling that the rival will prioritize delivery. The rival must
decide: deliver the commandeered cargo (gives the harbormaster points but earns you
influence) or neglect it (denies them points but costs you a constraint token).
The cargo token sitting on your ship mat is visible evidence of someone else's power
over you — and everyone at the table can see it.

## Artifact-as-story

The **manifest cards** are the physical centerpiece. Each card carries: a named party
(the Widow Aldren, the guild, House Vern), a good type, a destination, a deadline round,
and a one-line context. They are not anonymous obligations — they are named debts with
faces. At setup they are shuffled into a draft stack; players draft in snake order,
choosing which constraints to own. Once drafted, your manifest is face-up in front of
you — your obligations are not hidden. What's hidden is your influence reserve and your
plan for navigating them.

The manifest is load-bearing because it creates asymmetry without requiring asymmetric
ships, and because named obligations create accountability the rules don't have to
enforce mechanically. The same boat under three different manifests produces three
different strategies. The constraint IS the design — not a modifier on top of a design.

Commandeered cargo tokens (from direct orders) sit visibly on your ship mat, distinct
from your own manifest cards. The table can read your ship's story at a glance: what
you chose, what was imposed on you, and what you're still carrying unfulfilled.

Unfulfilled manifest obligations accumulate as **constraint tokens** on your ship. At two
tokens, cargo capacity shrinks. At four, you cannot SAIL — you are dock-bound until you
clear obligations. The manifest punishes neglect in the Rosenberg tradition: the clock
runs whether you're ready or not.

## Inspiration / lineage

- **Rosenberg — Agricola (2007)** — manifest draft as occupation-card analog; feeding
  pressure transposed to cargo obligations; the clock as inescapable mechanism.
- **Rosenberg — Le Havre (2008)** — port as shared processing node; goods conversion
  chain as the economic spine; scarcity of production buildings echoed in berth scarcity.
- **Wallace — Brass: Birmingham (2018)** — river geography as strategic layer; network
  timing under shared infrastructure; the industrial arc visible by mid-game.
- **Lacerda — Vinhos Deluxe (2016)** — authority positions that shape the rules others
  play under; information asymmetry in what rivals are committed to.
- **Knizia — Modern Art (1992)** — contested valuation via authority: who controls the
  price-setting mechanism shapes everyone's economics.

## Target review in the TIGRIS pipeline

**Predicted earns:**
- **B3 Conversion Chain Depth** — raw goods → processed goods → finished market goods
  is a three-step chain minimum; Le Havre architecture applied to river context.
- **C3 Scarcity Bite** — berth slots, cargo holds, tidal windows, and the constraint-
  token penalty all create genuine scarcity pressure that bites at all player counts.
- **B1 System Gearing** — the sailing→influence→authority feedback loop is the core
  gearing: good sailing earns influence, influence earns authority, authority shapes
  the rules that affect sailing. A closed loop.
- **A4 Variance Calibration** — manifest draft at setup + harbormaster rule cards +
  market event deck produces a different constraint configuration every game.

**Predicted refutes:**
- **B5 Architectural Novelty** — constraint-drafting as a primary mechanism is genuinely
  underexplored (no canonical Gap 3 game does this), but the Gap 3 claim rests on the
  combination, not any single element. Refute risk is moderate.
- **C4 Engine-Garden Dependency** — ships are symmetric; engine-garden dependency
  requires building-conditional value which symmetric ships may not deliver.

**Watch:**
- **D2 Spatial Interaction** — river geography creates spatial decisions; whether it
  earns depends on how strongly position matters vs. just cargo type.
- **A29 Forgiveness Curve** — manifest pressure is deliberately punishing (Rosenberg
  tradition); A29 will likely score at the punishing end (3–4), consistent with the
  ESTUARY and RITE data points.

## Non-goals

- Not a racing game. River movement is logistical, not competitive speed.
- Not a negotiation game. Harbormaster authority is mechanical; the direct-order power
  is exercised at influence cost, not through social contract.
- Not a deck-builder. The manifest draft happens once at setup; there is no ongoing
  deck construction or card cycling.
- Not an asymmetric-faction game. Ships are symmetric by design; variety from constraint
  configuration, not from engine asymmetry.
- Not a trading game. Goods are produced and delivered, not traded between players.

## Open questions (to resolve during DESIGN)

- **Rule card deck:** how many rule cards, what types? Minimum viable kit: berth
  priority (4 variants) + tidal window (2 variants) + customs rate (4 variants) +
  repair allocation (1). Total ~11. Is this enough variety across 5 rounds?
- **Manifest obligation failure:** constraint tokens accumulate, but what is the
  end-game cost of uncleared tokens? VP penalty, influence loss, or both?
- **Conversion chain specifics:** how many good types (suggest 4: timber, grain,
  wool, fish), how many processing steps per type, what equipment/building enables
  each step?
- **Harbormaster tenure:** resets each round (clean) or persists until challenged
  (creates momentum)? Persists-until-challenged rewards good sailing streaks but
  may create runaway authority.
- **2p calibration:** with only two players, Dock/Sail becomes a pure prisoner's
  dilemma (if both Dock, nobody sails; if both Sail, nobody rules). Does this work
  at 2p or does the game need a 3p minimum?
- **Direct order cost:** how much influence does issuing a direct order cost? Must
  be high enough that it's not spammed but low enough that it's used.
- **Market event deck:** how many events, how often do they fire, what types? Risk
  of adding too much randomness to a constraint-architecture game.
- **Commandeering incentive calibration:** the carrier earns influence for delivering
  commandeered cargo; the harbormaster scores VP. Is the influence reward high enough
  that carriers usually deliver? Or should neglecting commandeered cargo have a harsher
  penalty to ensure the mechanic fires reliably?
- **Named card count:** how many distinct named parties in the manifest deck? Too few
  and the same names repeat (weakens the personality). Too many and no name is
  memorable. Suggest 12–16 named parties for a deck of ~30 manifest cards.
