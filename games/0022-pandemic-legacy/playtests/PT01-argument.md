# PT01 — Pandemic Legacy Season 1: Argument Record

Narrated campaign playthrough at 4 players. Moment-anchors: Session 1 (fresh state), Sessions 3-4 (first legacy events), Sessions 7-8 (mid-campaign; significant state evolution), Sessions 11-12 (climax; maximum complexity). Standard Pandemic rules for Sessions 1-2; increasing legacy overlay thereafter.

---

## Moment-Anchor 1 — Session 1 (4-player, fresh state)

**Setup**: Standard Pandemic board. 4 players: Medic, Scientist, Dispatcher, Operations Expert. Epidemic cards: 5 (standard difficulty). No stickers applied. All legacy boxes sealed.

**Play summary**: Players open with role-coordination discussion. Medic focuses Asia (Red), Dispatcher enables rapid repositioning, Scientist accelerates cures. Standard Pandemic tension: infection rate 2, 2 outbreaks by Session 1 turn 8 (Black reaches 3 cubes in Cairo). Players win in Session 1 (4 cures found, turn 14).

**Legacy phase (post-Session 1)**: Win result → draw 2 legacy deck cards → unlock funding-track bonus. First city upgrade available. Medic character receives relationship card.

---

**A1 (Knizia) — Hold**: "Session 1 demonstrates Pandemic's elegant base: 4 actions × 4-disease-color × single-chain outbreak rule held perfectly. No complexity debt visible yet. Holding — final verdict after Session 12."

**B2 (Feld) — Defend**: "Epidemic #2 occurs on turn 6. Reshuffle of discard (intensify) re-places Cairo at top of infection deck. Black cubes cascade to 3 in Session 1 turn 8 — near-outbreak avoided only by Medic's 2-cube-per-treat ability. This is textbook B2 catastrophe-pressure: the epidemic mechanism operates exactly as designed. +1 defend on B2."

**D1 (K-K) — Hold**: "Session 1 is family-accessible as predicted. Full rules taught in 15 min. Holding for Sessions 8+ to confirm the scaling direction."

**A3 (Chvátil) — Defend**: "4-player action economy: 4 actions × 4 players = 16 total actions per full round. Session 1 turn 6 requires Dispatcher to use Airlift to reposition Medic to Cairo — a cooperation event not possible in a solo-player context. Mandatory interaction confirmed. +1 defend on A3."

**B5 (Chvátil) — Hold**: "Legacy content sealed through Session 1. Novelty of the mechanism latent. Deferring B5 defense to Sessions 3-4 (first legacy events)."

**B4 (Lacerda) — Defend**: "Sealed Boxes 2-5 present on the table. Players explicitly instructed not to look at contents. The information locked in Box 3 is inaccessible and will alter Session 7+ rules. This is a real information cost. +1 defend on B4."

**D4 (Rosenberg) — Defend**: "Legacy phase: players apply first funding-track sticker. The sticker is permanent. Turn-13 win triggers a specific legacy-deck draw that permanently modifies the Blue disease event rules. +1 defend on D4."

## Moment-Anchor 2 — Sessions 3-4 (mid-early campaign; first major legacy events)

**Setup**: Board now has 3 stickers (2 city upgrades, 1 funding bonus). Character scars: Medic has 1 scar from Session 2 bite (−1 hand-size). One city (Seoul) at Panic Level 1.

**Play summary**: Session 3 loss (Black spreads too fast; Cairo cascades to Seoul outbreak chain; 8th outbreak occurs on turn 11). Loss legacy-phase: Seoul advances to Panic Level 2. Legacy deck draw reveals Box 1 unlock (City Panic escalation rules). New rule stickered onto board: Panic-3 cities now infect differently. Session 4: win (3 turns to spare; players change strategy based on new panic rules).

---

**B5 (Chvátil anchor) — Defend**: "Box 1 unlock during Session 3 post-loss reveals a sealed rule that physically changes the behavior of Panic-3 cities. The rule was inaccessible 3 sessions ago. The game is architecturally distinct from any design that doesn't use permanent modification: the unlock event is a structural change to the rules, not a flavor event. +1 defend on B5."

**D4 (Rosenberg) — Defend**: "Seoul is now at Panic Level 2. This is permanent — no mechanism exists to de-escalate panic levels once set. The lock-in point is now evident: the Session 3 loss is literally encoded in the board state. +1 defend on D4."

**D1 (K-K) — Defend**: "Session 3 introduced the Panic-3 rule from Box 1 — a new rule layer that Session 1 players did not have to learn. Session 4 players need to know: base Pandemic rules + Panic levels 1-2 behavior + new Panic-3 rules. Complexity has increased. The scaling direction confirmed: accessibility is decreasing with each session. +1 defend on D1."

**B4 (Lacerda) — Defend**: "Box 2 remains sealed. During Session 4, players are warned (via a locked card visible through the box window) that 'something' is in Box 2. They know it exists but cannot access it. This is information cost at maximum: the information is visible enough to tantalize but withheld enough to prevent planning. +1 defend on B4."

**C1 (Knizia) — Defend**: "Session 3 outbreak cascade (Cairo → Seoul at turn 11) demonstrates the tension budget's peak operation: turn 11 of a 14-turn game had 4 simultaneous disease-state decisions requiring immediate triage. The epidemic-spaced-shuffle ensures this peak comes at roughly the same campaign position each game. +1 defend on C1."

**A2 (Feld) — Defend**: "Session 4, turn 7: Dispatcher must choose between (a) repositioning Medic to treat Seoul Panic-2 (avoiding Panic-3 next session) or (b) flying to Seoul to share a Black card for cure-set completion. The legacy-consequence of choosing (a) now enters the per-turn action-decision tree. This is a density increase not present in base Pandemic. +1 defend on A2."

**A7 (Lacerda) — Hold**: "Sessions 3-4 demonstrate emergence: the board state is genuinely different from any other copy of Pandemic Legacy due to Seoul's panic escalation. Holding full A7 defense to Sessions 7-8 where the no-replayability paradox will be most stark."

## Moment-Anchor 3 — Sessions 7-8 (mid-campaign; maximum divergence point)

**Setup**: Board has 12 stickers (4 city upgrades, 5 panic markers, 3 character modifications). Two characters have scars. Seoul is Panic Level 3 (fallen). Box 3 unlocked — new disease type introduced (Faded, with unique cube-behavior). 3 rules crossed out from the original rulebook and replaced with sticker-rules.

**Play summary**: Session 7 win (players used Box 3 unlock strategically; Faded disease is manageable but requires new resource allocation). Session 8 loss (Faded city outbreak chain from Mumbai to Tehran to Istanbul — 3-city cascade in turns 8-9; 8th outbreak reached). Post-Session 8 loss legacy: Istanbul joins Panic Level 2, 2 new Faded cities added.

---

### B5↔A7 COLLISION (Contested-Resolution, 5-3 for Chvátil; v2.20 CR)

**B5 (Chvátil anchor) — attacks A7**: "Session 7 opened Box 3, revealing a sealed disease type (Faded) that did not exist in the game until this moment. The Faded mechanism was literally inaccessible before Session 7. This is the architectural novelty that defines the legacy mechanism: the game is not the same game it was in Session 1. However — this is also why A7 Emergence-Replayability cannot earn in this context. The A7 axis requires BOTH emergence AND replayability. The 'emergence' half is trivially present (every session is unique). But Pandemic Legacy has zero replayability. After Session 12, this copy of the game is a record artifact, not a replayable game. The game is designed as a consumable experience. A7 staking this game says 'this game is replayable' — it is not. +1 defend on B5, attack on A7."

**A7 (Lacerda) — defends against B5 attack**: "The campaign arc is emergence in its purest form: Session 1 produces a state that Session 2 inherits; Session 2 modifies what Session 3 can become. The state-divergence across 12 sessions is more extreme than any single-game emergence in the corpus. The 'replayability' component of A7 is satisfied by the campaign's re-entry across sessions: you replay (re-engage) the same system 12 times, each time with a new starting state. The 'one campaign per copy' constraint doesn't destroy replayability — it redefines it."

**Chvátil: rebuttal**: "Lacerda's interpretation of 'replayability' as 'campaign re-entry across sessions' is a redefinition of the axis. A7's canonical earning condition is: play state diverges across multiple PLAYS of the same game, maintaining interest. The campaign sessions are not 'plays of the same game' — they are a sequential narrative. You cannot play Session 7 without having played Sessions 1-6. And after Session 12, you cannot play Session 1 again on this copy. A7 measures whether a design sustains long-term interest through play-state divergence. Legacy does this exactly once per copy — that is the definition of non-replayability."

**Panel vote (CR — which stake better characterizes the Session 7-8 game state?):**
- Rosenberg → Chvátil: "D4 lock-in proves irreversibility; the campaign sessions are serialized, not replayable."
- Feld → Chvátil: "B2 catastrophe is the strongest signal — the Faded disease from Box 3 is a catastrophe designed to be experienced once and never again. The one-shot design is architectural."
- K-K → Lacerda: "D1 scaling argument says the 12-session arc is a form of replayability — you keep returning to the same system."
- Stegmaier → Lacerda: "A6 teachability note: A7 as 'campaign arc emergence' is a coherent reading."
- Vaccarino → Chvátil: "A4 variance: the epidemic-spaced-shuffle guarantees variance per session — but it's variance within a one-time arc, not replayability."
- Knizia → Chvátil: "A1 elegance argument: A7's canonical definition requires both components. A design that earns only half of a 2-component axis earns the refutation on the other half."

**Result: 4-2 for Chvátil (plus Knizia = 5-2 effective; K-K and Stegmaier dissent). B5 earns collision credit. A7 takes collision refutation.** A7: 8E/0R → 8E/1R. First post-v2.19-recovery refutation.

New players who joined at Session 7 would face an undocumented game — no single rulebook describes this board. The Session 1 'family accessible' entry point is now 6 sessions behind the current state. The scaling gradient is exactly as steep as D1 predicts. +1 defend on D1."

**D2 (K-K) — Defend**: "Session 8 loss was caused by Mumbai → Tehran → Istanbul Faded cascade, a 3-city geographic chain along the eastern Mediterranean city-graph edge. D2 spatial mechanism is the game's loss engine. +1 defend on D2."

**B1 (Rosenberg) — Defend**: "Session 8 turn 9: infection-rate = 4. Epidemic #4 fires. Intensify reshuffles the discard pile (now including Mumbai, Tehran, Istanbul) onto the top of the infection deck — ensuring these same cities infect again in turns 10-11. The 5-gear cascade (infection deck → epidemic → intensify → infection-rate-4 next draw → cascade) operated exactly as the gearing predicts. +1 defend on B1."

## Moment-Anchor 4 — Sessions 11-12 (endgame; maximum legacy state)

**Setup**: Board has 22 stickers. 4 cities fallen (Panic Level 3). 8 cities are Faded-overlay. 2 characters retired from wounds; 2 new characters introduced from legacy unlock. Original rulebook has 7 crossed-out rules replaced by sticker-rules. Setup time for Session 11: ~22 minutes of board-reading, sticker-confirming, and legacy-history review.

**Play summary**: Session 11 win (players cracked the endgame sequence; used the relationship mechanic introduced in Session 9). Session 12 (final): win by the last turn (player deck had 3 cards remaining; 2 outbreaks pending but blocked).

**Legacy phase final**: All remaining boxes opened. Campaign ends.

### A6↔C8 COLLISION (Orthogonal-Preservation, 6-2 for OP; v2.20 OP)

**C8 (K-K) — fires at Session 11 setup**: "22-minute setup for Session 11. The first-turn compression has completely inverted: Session 1 had 5 minutes of setup; Session 11 has 22 minutes. C8 measures session-opening overhead — this is the worst setup-to-session-length ratio in the TIGRIS corpus. +1 defend on C8."

**A6 (Stegmaier) — fires at Session 11 entry**: "A6 asks: can I teach this to new players? New players cannot join at Session 11 — the board state is undocumented for anyone who wasn't there for Sessions 1-10. But A6's earning condition was satisfied at Session 1, where the game is a teaching standard. The axis asking 'can this be taught?' and the axis asking 'how much overhead does session-opening take?' are fundamentally different questions. Proposing OP: A6 measures rule-communication to new players (relevant at Session 1); C8 measures session-setup overhead (relevant throughout but maximally at Sessions 10-12). These are not the same question."

**K-K (C8) seconds OP**: "Session 1 is A6's domain. Sessions 11-12 are C8's domain. The same game at different moments in its arc hits two different axes — not the same moment twice."

**Panel vote on OP:**
- Rosenberg → OP: "A6 and C8 are asking about different temporal slices of the campaign arc — entry vs. established."
- Feld → OP: "B2's catastrophe-pressure argument reinforces: the campaign is one thing across 12 sessions, but A6 fires at session-entry and C8 fires at session-setup. Orthogonal temporal registers."
- Lacerda → OP: "B4 information-cost argument: the information-cost of the sealed boxes (B4) is orthogonal to both A6 and C8. Three axes can fire on different registers of the same game without subsumption."
- Chvátil → CR: "A6 and C8 both address 'how hard is it to start playing?' — same question, different components. CR should determine which better characterizes the entry obstacle."
- Knizia → OP: "A6 is about rule communication to a new player. C8 is about overhead for an existing player group. 'New player' vs 'existing player group' is a different subject population — genuinely orthogonal."
- Stegmaier → OP (proposer).
- Vaccarino → OP.

**Result: 6-2 for OP (Chvátil + one other dissent). Both A6 and C8 earn defend marks.** First OP-collision in TIGRIS involving temporal-register orthogonality (different campaign phases as orthogonal registers). v2.20 OP classification applies.
